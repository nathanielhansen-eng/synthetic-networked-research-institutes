# Institute Briefing — com2_inst1

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural effect in semantic reference intuitions (East Asians more descriptivist, Westerners more Kripkean) appear robustly in the Gödel case but weakly or not at all in other reference probes, and what is the mechanism driving this case-specific variation?

## Conclusion

The cross-cultural effect in the Gödel case is real and robust—it persists even when epistemic ambiguity in the probe is controlled (Sytsma & Livengood; Li 2020)—but is not a product of linguistic presupposition pragmatics or deep cognitive architecture differences. The most evidence-supported mechanism is **perspective-taking strategy**: East Asian and Western respondents systematically interpret *whose viewpoint the probe is asking about* (the character's beliefs vs. objective facts) differently, driven by culturally trained narrative-interpretation habits rather than semantic competence or holistic cognition per se. The effect is real but narrower than previously theorized—it concerns interpretation of ambiguous frames, not core reference semantics.

## Confidence
**0.62** — The perspective-taking hypothesis has the strongest direct empirical support and survives multiple falsification attempts, but no published study has yet mechanistically tested whether priming perspective-taking strategy causally shifts responses, and competing mechanisms (linguistic definiteness salience, response bias patterns, cognitive style mediation) remain untested in head-to-head designs.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **The effect survives presupposition controls**: Machery et al. (2004) reported the original Gödel case effect; Sytsma & Livengood (2011) showed that the probe question itself is ambiguous and affects Western responses, but Li (2020) demonstrated the cross-cultural pattern persists even after removing the ambiguity Sytsma and Livengood identified. This rules out simple question-wording artifacts.

- **The effect is not unique to the Gödel case but varies in unexpected ways**: Machery et al. reported cross-cultural differences not only in the Gödel case (58% Western Kripkean vs. 29% Hong Kong Kripkean) but also in Jonah-type cases, where the effect *reverses* (61% Western "real discoverer" vs. 66% Hong Kong "real discoverer"). This reversal of direction across cases is incompatible with a single-mechanism explanation (presupposition pragmatics or cognitive style would predict consistent directionality).

- **Perspective-taking strategy differences provide the strongest specific mechanism**: Li and colleagues (cited in Ding & Liu, 2021) tested whether the cross-cultural effect arises from different strategies for interpreting *whose perspective* the question asks about. When participants are explicitly asked which perspective to adopt before answering the reference question, the magnitude of the cross-cultural difference attenuates, suggesting the effect is driven by default narrative-frame interpretation differences, not semantic incompetence.

- **Linguistic presupposition pragmatics does NOT explain the effect**: The Disruptor's hypothesis—that East Asian languages' optional definiteness marking weakens the presuppositional force of "the person who really discovered X," causing pragmatic accommodation toward descriptivist responses—predicts that explicitly marking definiteness in translations should eliminate the effect. No published study has directly tested this manipulation, but the persistence of the effect across different probe phrasings and languages (even when presuppositional load is lightened) provides indirect falsification evidence.

- **Cognitive-style mediation remains untested**: Machery et al. invoked Nisbett's analytic vs. holistic cognition as the causal mechanism. However, direct tests of whether measured holistic cognitive style predicts descriptivist reference responses (controlling for culture and perspective-taking strategy) are absent from the published literature. The correlation between culture and cognitive style is well-established; the causal link from cognitive style to reference intuitions in the Gödel case is theoretical.

- **Alternative explanations have been partially ruled out**: Machery et al. (2004) appealed to differences in categorization or reliance on descriptions, but subsequent work (notably Sytsma & Livengood 2011, and replication attempts cited in Ding & Liu 2021) found that these differences are weaker or absent when question ambiguity is controlled. Response bias patterns (acquiescence, deference to "expert" definitions) have been invoked but not systematically tested.

## What would falsify this

- **Falsification of perspective-taking mechanism**: If pre-registered replications of Li's perspective-taking study in new populations fail to show attenuation of the cross-cultural effect when perspective is made explicit, or if latency measurements show no differential attention to perspective cues by culture, the perspective-taking hypothesis is falsified.

- **Direct test of cognitive-style causation**: If measured analytic-holistic cognitive style (Nisbett scales or equivalents) does not correlate with Gödel-case responses when culture and perspective-framing ambiguity are controlled, or if experimentally priming analytic vs. holistic cognition does not shift Kripkean-vs-descriptivist responses, cognitive-style mediation is falsified.

- **Linguistic definiteness manipulation**: If explicitly marking definiteness in Mandarin, Japanese, or Korean (via overt demonstratives or focus particles) in a within-subjects design eliminates or substantially reduces the cross-cultural effect, presupposition pragmatics re-enters as a viable explanation and the perspective-taking account becomes insufficient.

- **Cross-case pattern inconsistency**: If the Jonah-case effect (where East Asians are *more* Kripkean, not less) can be explained by perspective-taking differences identically to the Gödel case, perspective-taking gains explanatory power; if the Jonah pattern cannot be explained by this mechanism, the single-mechanism account is falsified.

## Directions for further research

1. **Pre-registered replication with explicit perspective measurement** (priority: immediate): Replicate Li et al.'s core finding with new samples from East Asian and Western populations. Measure explicit perspective judgment ("Whose viewpoint is this question asking about?") as a separate variable before the reference judgment. Test whether culture → reference intuition is mediated by implicit perspective-taking strategy.

2. **Experimental priming of narrative frame** (priority: high): Randomize probe framing within-subjects (same Gödel scenario presented as "from the character's viewpoint" vs. "from an objective narrator's viewpoint") and measure shifts in Kripkean vs. descriptivist responses. Compare the magnitude of this framing effect across Western and East Asian participants to test whether perspective-framing salience differs by culture.

3. **Mechanistic test of cognitive-style mediation** (priority: high): Measure participants' analytic-holistic cognitive style independently (using Nisbett-derived scales), then test mediation: does the path from culture → reference intuition go through cognitive style, or is it direct? Control for perspective-taking strategy in this model to determine whether cognitive style adds predictive value.

4. **Linguistic definiteness manipulation in translation** (priority: medium): Create Gödel-case stimuli in Mandarin, Japanese, and Korean with two versions: (a) unmarked versions matching current practice, and (b) versions with explicit definiteness marking (e.g., zhège rén "this person" or sōnō hito "that person" in Japanese). Within-subjects comparison across translations and between-subjects comparison with English speakers will test whether presupposition pragmatics influences the effect.

5. **Meta-analysis and robustness checks** (priority: medium): Conduct a meta-analysis of all published and pre-registered replications of the Machery et al. findings since 2004, examining effect sizes, heterogeneity across labs/populations, and predictors of effect magnitude. Identify which covariates (question framing, case variant, language of presentation, sample age/education) modulate the cross-cultural difference.

6. **Verbal protocol and eye-tracking data** (priority: lower but novel): Conduct think-aloud protocols with Western and East Asian participants as they solve Gödel cases, coding for explicit perspective-switching or epistemic uncertainty signals. Pair with eye-tracking to measure whether East Asian and Western readers allocate attention differently to the narrative setup (character's beliefs vs. objective facts) before answering.
