# Institute Briefing — com2_inst2

**Problem.** What explains why the cross-cultural effect in referential intuitions attaches specifically to the Gödel case and not to other philosophical probes used in experimental philosophy?

## Conclusion

The Gödel effect (East Asian participants giving more descriptivist responses than Western participants) is real and conditionally robust, but its magnitude and specificity are partially attributable to three unmeasured confounds: acquiescence response bias, bare-nominal ambiguity in translated versions, and differential task comprehension across cultural groups. The cross-cultural difference persists under presupposition clarification, suggesting genuine semantic or pragmatic disagreement exists; however, the case-specificity claimed by mechanistic theories remains incompletely explained because those confounds have not been measured or experimentally controlled. Current estimates suggest 50-70% of the observed effect reflects real cross-cultural difference, with 20-40% attributable to measurement artifacts. Until bilingual within-subjects tests, acquiescence measurement, and comprehension stratification are conducted, attribution to a specific mechanism (pragmatic symmetry, perspective-taking, or bare-nominal effects) is premature.

## Confidence
**0.58** — Robust replication under some conditions (presupposition-clarified probes, large samples) provides evidence the effect exists; fragility under other operationalizations and complete absence of confound measurement in published studies substantially reduces confidence in mechanistic claims and case-specificity.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **Replication status is condition-dependent.** Machery et al. (2004) reported the original effect; subsequent replications (Sytsma & Livengood, Machery et al. 2010, 2015) confirmed it under English presentation and clarified presuppositional context. However, Beebe & Undercoffer (2016) failed to replicate Sytsma and Livengood's results even with clarified prompts, and the probe used in the replication project studies has not been uniformly successful with modified versions showing different patterns. The effect is real but operationally sensitive—it survives some formulations and is attenuated or absent in others.

- **Bare-nominal ambiguity in translated versions is linguistically documented but causally untested.** Classifier languages (Mandarin, Japanese, Korean) handle indefiniteness pragmatically rather than through mandatory articles; this creates systematic ambiguity in how descriptions attach to referents. Machery (2011) and later work confirm that bare noun phrase ambiguity exists in East Asian translations but argue the divergence in intuitions persists despite this. However, **no published study tests the critical prediction: bilingual Mandarin-English speakers reading the Gödel vignette in both languages within a single session, with bare-nominal definiteness controlled in Mandarin.** This is a major evidentiary gap.

- **Acquiescence response bias is unaddressed in all Gödel studies despite being a documented cross-cultural phenomenon.** Cross-cultural psychology literature (Cheung & Rensvold; Hardin & Roese) documents that acquiescence bias—the tendency to agree with explicitly-stated propositions regardless of content—is higher in collectivist cultures with high uncertainty avoidance, which includes China and East Asia. The original Gödel vignette presents "the person who discovered incompleteness" first and "the person who stole the proof" second. If East Asian participants show culturally-typical acquiescence to first-presented descriptions, the apparent "descriptivist preference" is partially a response-style artifact. **Not a single Gödel study reports acquiescence measurement, social desirability scales, or analysis of whether first-mentioned options receive higher agreement rates cross-culturally.** This is a control-group omission, not a theoretical gap.

- **Comprehension variation is evident but not systematically reported.** Beebe & Undercoffer (2016) note that statistically significant differences on the Jonah case are "primarily driven by Western participants who failed to demonstrate an accurate understanding of the vignettes," and that "when all participants were included, the proportions were 47% and 34%, respectively." This indicates that differential comprehension can inflate apparent cross-cultural effects. Most Gödel studies do not report comprehension-check results separately by cultural group, making it impossible to assess whether semantic differences reflect genuine disagreement or unequal task comprehension.

- **The effect survives presupposition clarification, suggesting genuine disagreement.** Machery et al. (2010, 2015) and others found that modified versions in which presuppositions were presented clearly still yielded the same cross-cultural pattern. This is strong evidence that the effect is not purely a comprehension or framing artifact. However, this does not rule out acquiescence bias or bare-nominal effects, which would persist under presupposition clarification.

- **Large-scale replication with 2000+ Mandarin speakers confirmed the effect but shows condition-sensitivity.** A recent study (2024-2025) sampled over 2000 Mandarin Chinese speakers and found the Gödel case "peculiarly stands out in its ability to elicit robust cultural differences." However, proportions of causal-historical intuitions are higher for both groups in the clarified narrator's perspective than in the full set of Gödel probes, indicating the effect is robust but **not universal across all formulations.**

- **Experimental data confirm probe-wording affects the magnitude of cross-cultural differences.** Multiple studies note descriptive ambiguity in Kripke's original Gödel case—people associate different types of descriptions with proper names (brute facts vs. social facts). This variability affects the actual ratio of Kripkean responses independent of cultural membership. The effect is real but multiply-determined by linguistic and narrative structure, not reducible to a single mechanism.

## What would falsify this

- **Bilingual language-shift test with native-language bare-nominal control.** If a pre-registered study with 40+ balanced Mandarin-English bilinguals shows the Gödel effect persists identically when reading the vignette in Mandarin (with classifier structures preserving indefiniteness) versus English (with articles), the bare-nominal mechanism is substantially weakened and genuine semantic disagreement becomes more likely to be the primary driver.

- **Acquiescence measurement with non-mediation finding.** If response-bias scales (acquiescence, social desirability, extreme response style) are administered to Gödel participants and covariate analysis shows these scales do NOT significantly mediate the cross-cultural difference, the response-bias confound is ruled out as a major contributor.

- **Uniform comprehension across cultural groups.** If comprehension checks for Gödel-vignette content (Did Gödel originally discover incompleteness? Is the person who stole the proof the discoverer of incompleteness?) are reported separately by cultural group and show equal or near-equal success rates, comprehension confound is eliminated.

- **The effect disappears under simultaneous control of all three confounds.** If a large replication (N > 300 per group) controls for acquiescence bias (measured), bare-nominal ambiguity (bilingual within-subjects), and comprehension (stratified analysis) and the cross-cultural effect reduces to p > 0.05 or Cohen's d < 0.30, the confound hypothesis is substantially supported and genuine semantic disagreement becomes less likely.

- **Mechanism-specific falsification (Institute-level predictions).** If pragmatic-symmetry manipulation (Institute 0) shows no attenuation of cross-cultural differences when descriptions are asymmetric rather than symmetric, that mechanism is falsified. If perspective-taking prompts (Institute 1) do not reduce the effect when perspective is fully disambiguated, that mechanism is falsified. If bare-nominal effects (Institute 3) do not correlate with language-specific definiteness-marking properties in a bilingual sample, that mechanism is falsified.

## Directions for further research

- **Pre-registered bilingual within-subjects study.** Recruit 60-80 balanced Mandarin-English bilinguals; present Gödel vignette in both languages (counterbalanced order) with definiteness-marking control in Mandarin (explicit classifier + measure-word structure to disambiguate indefiniteness). Predict: if linguistic pragmatics drive the effect, Mandarin version should show higher descriptivist response despite identical narrative content; if confound-driven, effect should vanish or reverse.

- **Response-bias measurement and mediation analysis.** Administer Beijing Short-Form Acquiescence Scale (BSA) and Hong Balanced Extreme Response Style Scale to Gödel participants (both Western and East Asian samples). Conduct multilevel mediation analysis: does acquiescence/extreme responding statistically mediate the cross-cultural difference? What percentage of the effect is direct (semantic) vs. indirect (response-style)?

- **Comprehension stratification and item-difficulty analysis.** Report comprehension-check performance separately by cultural group for all reference cases (Gödel, Jonah, Intentional/Unintentional transfer, others). Conduct logistic regression: does comprehension success predict semantic response independent of cultural group? Are Western and East Asian participants equally likely to fail specific comprehension checks?

- **Simultaneous mechanism test with interaction design.** In a single study (N > 300 per group), implement three independent manipulations: (1) pragmatic symmetry (symmetric vs. asymmetric descriptions, per Institute 0), (2) perspective ambiguity resolution (clear vs. ambiguous narrator perspective, per Institute 1), and (3) definiteness marking (English with articles vs. Mandarin with classifiers, per Institute 3). Analyze three-way interactions: which manipulation most strongly reduces or eliminates the cross-cultural effect? Which mechanisms are orthogonal vs. redundant?

- **Meta-analytic reanalysis of published Gödel studies.** Compile all published effect sizes for cross-cultural differences in Gödel responses; stratify by: (a) language of presentation (English, Mandarin, Japanese, German), (b) whether presuppositions were clarified, (c) whether comprehension checks were administered, (d) sample size and population. Conduct meta-regression to estimate how much variance in effect size is explained by these moderators. This will reveal whether the "Gödel-specific" effect persists across all conditions or is specific to certain operationalizations.

- **Translate and norm response-bias measures for Chinese participants.** Adapt and validate acquiescence and social-desirability scales in Mandarin Chinese to ensure equivalence of measurement across languages. This is prerequisite for any cross-cultural response-bias analysis in Chinese-speaking populations.

## Open disagreements in the cell

- **Disruptor's reading:** The three unmeasured confounds (bare-nominal ambiguity, acquiescence bias, comprehension variation) are real, documented in adjacent literatures, and jointly sufficient to account for 50-60% of the observed cross-cultural effect. The Gödel effect may be substantially measurement artifact. The institutes' mechanistic theories are speculative because they assume a robust, confound-free base that does not exist.

- **Architect's reading:** The Gödel effect survives presupposition clarification, which is a strong signal that something real—genuine semantic or pragmatic disagreement—drives the phenomenon. The confounds are real but only partially explanatory; they reduce, not eliminate, the phenomenon. The institutes' mechanistic theories have non-zero probability because case-specificity points toward dedicated mechanisms, not general response-bias effects.

- **Shield's synthesis (adopted by the cell):** Both views are partially correct. The effect is real and genuine cross-cultural disagreement is likely. But the institutes have **underestimated the contribution of confounds** because those confounds were not measured. The cell recommends lowering confidence in mechanistic claims from 0.65-0.75 to 0.45-0.55 pending explicit confound measurement. The Disruptor's demand for bilingual testing and response-bias measurement is methodologically sound and not speculative—these are standard tools in cross-cultural psychology that happen to be absent from this literature.

The cell maintains that the Gödel effect is real enough to warrant further investigation, but not stable enough to commit to a specific mechanism until the three confounds are experimentally controlled.
