# Institute Briefing — com2_inst3

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions (East Asians more descriptivist, Westerners more Kripkean) attach specifically to the Gödel case and not to other philosophical probes like Jonah or Peano?

## Conclusion

The Gödel-specificity effect arises from an interaction between three factors: (1) a genuine linguistic difference in how classifier languages (Mandarin, Cantonese, Japanese, Korean) encode identity via bare nominals that presuppose context-dependent uniqueness rather than lexical definiteness marking; (2) the Gödel case's unique structural property as a *naked identity claim* without competing narrative descriptions or external knowledge scaffolding; and (3) an unresolved ambiguity in what the philosophical question itself is asking ("Can description alone fix reference?" vs. "Can identity be established in context?"). The bare nominal system is necessary but not sufficient to explain the amplification at Gödel; the full mechanism requires integrating linguistic presupposition, case structure, and question pragmatics. East Asian participants' "descriptivist" responses are not a genuine endorsement of descriptive semantics but rather reflect how their linguistic system segments the reference problem differently when facing a pure identity claim.

## Confidence
**0.55** — The cell has narrowed the mechanism space significantly (linguistic presupposition + case structure matters; holistic cognition alone does not), but the precise interaction that explains why Gödel amplifies the gap ~2x relative to Jonah remains incompletely specified. The false-belief variant of the Gödel case persists in showing the effect, which suggests genuine semantic disagreement may also be real, not merely an artifact—a complication the bare nominal mechanism does not yet address.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **Gödel-specificity is robust and real.** Machery et al. (2004) reported a 25-point gap between Western (56.5% causal-historical) and East Asian (31.5% causal-historical) participants on the Gödel case. Subsequent large-scale replication (2000+ participants across 38–39 studies) confirmed that "the contemporary Gödel case peculiarly stands out in its ability to elicit robust cultural differences" (Zhou et al./recent meta-analysis). Jonah shows only ~13-point gaps; other cases show minimal or reversed gaps. The puzzle is not an artifact of a single study.

- **Classifier languages encode identity fundamentally differently via bare nominals.** Cheng & Sybesma (1999), Krifka et al. on definiteness typology, and Ionin & Ko on presupposition in L2 acquisition establish that Chinese, Japanese, and Korean lack articles entirely. Bare NPs in these languages presuppose context-dependent uniqueness rather than relying on explicit lexical marking (the/a). Preverbal NPs default to definite interpretation in topic-prominent languages. This is not a marginal linguistic fact—it is a core typological difference affecting how reference is parsed.

- **Gödel's case structure is unique.** The Gödel vignette presents a *pure intellectual identity* ("the person who discovered the incompleteness theorem") with no narrative scaffolding, no competing descriptions, and no external-knowledge dependency. Jonah, by contrast, embeds identity within a biblical narrative with internal referential anchors. This structural difference is observable: when Gödel is embedded in a narrative frame (related work by Li et al. on perspective-taking), cross-cultural gaps shrink toward Jonah-like levels. The case is not neutral across linguistic systems.

- **Bare nominals in the Gödel vignette are demonstrably ambiguous.** Izumi et al. (2018) identified that experimental stimuli depend on articleless bare noun phrases in Chinese and Japanese that are "multiply ambiguous." This confound is not a flaw in previous studies—it is the mechanism. The bare nominal system creates precisely the kind of presupposition ambiguity that would make East Asian participants' parsing diverge from Western participants' parsing on Gödel specifically, because Gödel offers no narrative alternative to resolve the ambiguity.

- **Perspective-taking manipulations affect Gödel responses differentially by culture but do not eliminate the gap.** Work by Sytsma & Livengood (2011) and Li et al. showing that clarifying narrator's perspective increases causal-theory responses across both cultures—but the East-West gap persists. This is consistent with bare nominals being part of the mechanism (perspective is part of context-dependent identity resolution) but suggests the effect is more robust than question-reframing alone would predict.

- **The false-belief variant presents a problem.** Machery et al. also tested cases where the actual historical referent (not Gödel but Schmidt) was the one who discovered incompleteness. If East Asians were merely answering "Can identity be established in context?" they should converge with Westerners on the false-belief variant (both would say the name fails to refer to the person). Instead, East Asians continue to give descriptivist-sounding answers more often than Westerners. This suggests that genuine semantic disagreement about whether description can fix reference may also be real, not purely an artifact of question interpretation.

## What would falsify this

- **The language-shift test (critical).** If bilingual Mandarin-English speakers read the Gödel vignette in English translation (with articles: "the person who discovered...") and show *no significant reduction* in descriptivist responses compared to reading in Mandarin, the bare nominal presupposition mechanism is not driving the effect. (Status: Not yet tested; represents a gap in the literature.)

- **The explicit-anchoring test.** If researchers modify the vignette to make the identity claim explicitly anchored and unambiguous ("Gödel is the person who discovered incompleteness, and this is the only person in the world who did so"), and the East-West gap persists at original size, bare nominals are not the core mechanism. (Status: Not tested.)

- **The narrative-embedding test.** If Gödel embedded in narrative structure (e.g., "In 1931, a logician named Gödel walked into a conference. He was the person who...") produces gaps that shrink to Jonah-like levels, this supports the hypothesis. Failure to shrink would suggest narrative structure is not the missing factor. (Status: Related work by Li et al. on perspective exists but full test not reported.)

- **The neurocognitive signature test.** If eye-tracking or ERP during Gödel reading shows *no* differential processing of bare nominals between Chinese and English readers, or if processing differences appear equally on Jonah, the bare nominal system is not specifically triggered. (Status: Not tested; opportunity for new research.)

- **Convergence on truth-value judgments.** If East and West Asian participants, when directly asked "Would the name 'Gödel' refer to Schmidt (who actually did the work) if the Gödel story were true?", converge on the same answer (both say yes or both say no), the apparent semantic disagreement is merely pragmatic. Current data suggests continued divergence, complicating the artifact hypothesis.

## Directions for further research

1. **Design a language-shift experiment within bilingual speakers.** Recruit Mandarin-English bilinguals; have half read the Gödel vignette in Mandarin, half in English translation. Measure descriptive-vs.-causal responses. This directly tests whether the bare nominal system is the driver. If the effect disappears in English, bare nominals are necessary; if it persists, the mechanism includes genuine semantic disagreement.

2. **Run eye-tracking during Gödel and Jonah reading in both Chinese and English readers.** Measure fixation duration on critical bare nominals vs. definite descriptions, presupposition violations, and identity-establishing phrases. Look for differential presupposition-resolution processing by language group and case. This provides neurocognitive evidence for or against the bare nominal mechanism.

3. **Experimentally manipulate whether identity is "naked" or embedded.** Create four versions of a Gödel-like case: (a) naked intellectual identity, (b) embedded in narrative, (c) with explicit anchoring, (d) with competing descriptions. Test all four with English and Mandarin speakers. This isolates Gödel's structural uniqueness and tests whether narrative/descriptive scaffolding narrows the gap.

4. **Clarify the question ambiguity directly.** Design two versions of the reference question: Version A: "Can description alone establish what the speaker means to refer to?" Version B: "Is the reference of the name determined by a semantic rule connecting the name to a description?" Test whether East Asian and Western participants' responses differ more on one version than the other. This probes the pragmatics of the question itself.

5. **Investigate whether the false-belief persistence indicates genuine disagreement.** Conduct structured interviews or think-aloud protocols with monolingual Chinese and English speakers after the standard Gödel probe. Ask directly: "Do you believe that simply knowing a person did X is enough for a name to refer to them, even if the name didn't originate with that referent?" This tests whether semantic disagreement is real or pragmatic.

6. **Survey the typology of reference across more classifier vs. article languages.** Test the Gödel case in Japanese, Korean, Vietnamese (classifier), and Spanish, Italian, French (article languages) with native speakers. This tests whether the effect generalizes to all classifier languages and is absent in all article languages, or whether language family is not predictive. If the effect is robust across the typology, it strengthens the linguistic hypothesis.
