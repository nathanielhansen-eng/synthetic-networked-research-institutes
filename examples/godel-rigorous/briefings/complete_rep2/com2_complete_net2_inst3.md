# Institute Briefing — com2_inst3

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in intuitions about the Gödel case (Machery et al. 2004) attach specifically to that case and not consistently to other reference probes (Jonah, Twin Earth, Peano)?

## Conclusion

The cross-cultural difference reflects a **pragmatic-cognitive default in epistemic perspective resolution**, not a linguistic mechanism rooted in article systems or grammaticalized topic-prominence. When faced with competing descriptions in a belief-report frame where neither is pragmatically marked as canonical (pragmatic symmetry), East Asian participants systematically default to interpreting descriptions from the protagonist's epistemic stance (John's belief), while Western participants infer toward the narrator's objective reference. This perspective-taking strategy is culturally shaped, emerges early (by age 7), and generalizes to structurally similar cases—those combining vivid pragmatic symmetry, ambiguous epistemic perspective, and belief attribution. The Gödel case is distinctive because it uniquely presents all three features; other cases either lack pragmatic symmetry or have asymmetries that resolve the ambiguity in a single canonical direction.

## Confidence

**0.68**

This reflects support from Sytsma & Livengood (2011) on epistemic perspective ambiguity as a central feature; Li et al.'s perspective-taking studies cited in Ding & Liu (2021); and critically, Lam (2010), whose Cantonese study found **no East-West difference when ambiguity was reduced**, despite identical topic-prominence in both languages. This falsifies grammatical mechanisms (Disruptor's scope-interpretation hypothesis) while supporting pragmatic-cognitive mechanisms. However, confidence is not higher because: (a) the literature has not yet directly causally manipulated perspective-taking strategy to measure its effect; (b) individual-difference validation of perspective-taking tendency as a predictor within cultures is sparse; and (c) the precise cognitive process by which East Asian speakers default to protagonist perspective remains mechanistically underspecified.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **Epistemic perspective ambiguity is central, not article systems.** Machery et al. (2004) and later Machery (2015) explicitly acknowledge that the Gödel case admits two readings depending on whose epistemic perspective (John's or the narrator's) is adopted. However, Machery's presupposition-control experiments show that clarifying presuppositions does *not* eliminate the cross-cultural effect, falsifying the hypothesis that article absence forces East Asians to rely on explicit descriptions. The ambiguity persists as a *pragmatic* feature, not a grammatical necessity.

- **Lam (2010) on Cantonese: The definitive falsification of linguistic mechanisms.** When Cantonese-speaking participants were given a structurally parallel case to Gödel *with reduced pragmatic ambiguity*, the East-West difference **disappeared entirely**, despite Cantonese being topic-prominent (possessing the same grammatical structure the Disruptor hypothesized would cause de dicto freezing). This establishes that topic-prominence itself does not drive the effect. The effect is triggered by *pragmatic* ambiguity, not grammatical structure.

- **Pragmatic symmetry is the distinguishing feature.** The Gödel case presents two descriptions (the actual discoverer vs. the person John believes is the discoverer) with equal pragmatic salience and plausibility. Neither is marked as socially canonical or objectively preferable. The Jonah case, by contrast, uses a name ("Jonah") with established referential history; Twin Earth involves objective physical facts that collapse de re/de dicto; Peano involves a canonical historical figure. These cases either lack symmetry or resolve it asymmetrically, predicting weaker or absent cross-cultural effects—a prediction consistent with reported data showing the effect is strongest and most consistent for Gödel.

- **Early emergence by age 7 rules out complex folk-theory acquisition.** Institute 2 correctly notes the effect emerges early. However, this timeline is more consistent with narrative-interpretation habit formation (perspective-taking conventions in how stories are understood) than with the acquisition of explicit reference semantics. By age 7, children have acquired basic narrative perspective-taking; they are still developing more sophisticated scope interpretation. This timeline supports Institute 1's perspective-taking mechanism over Institute 2's folk-theory account.

- **The effect generalizes to structurally similar cases, not all cases.** Machery et al. (2004) report variation across probes, but Institute 2's claim of across-the-board generalization requires qualification. The cross-cultural effect is largest and most reliable in cases with pragmatic symmetry and belief attribution (structure matching Gödel). This predicts that the effect should generalize to other carefully constructed cases with identical structure—a prediction that can be tested but has not yet been systematically explored in the literature.

- **Individual and intra-cultural variation in scope interpretation is high.** Barrett et al.'s work cited in the cell discussion reveals that even within Mandarin Chinese, there is bimodal distribution of scope-interpretation preferences in doubly-quantified sentences. This variation is inconsistent with a grammaticalized, mandatory scope-freezing mechanism (Disruptor's hypothesis) and suggests instead that scope interpretation is a flexible cognitive process influenced by pragmatic context and individual strategy—supporting the pragmatic-cognitive mechanism.

- **Presupposition controls survive without triggering the folk-theory alternative.** Machery (2015) demonstrates that clarifying presuppositions does not eliminate the cross-cultural effect, and Sytsma & Livengood (2011) show the effect persists under various framings. This rules out pure pragmatic conservatism (Institute 0) as the sole mechanism. However, it does *not* require appeal to monolithic folk-theory differences. Instead, it suggests the effect is **robust to presupposition structure because it operates at the level of epistemic perspective**, which is orthogonal to presupposition clarity.

## What would falsify this

- **Perspective-taking strategy has no causal effect.** If an experiment explicitly primes participants toward either the narrator's perspective ("Consider the objective fact: who *really* discovered incompleteness?") or John's perspective ("From John's viewpoint: who does he believe?"), and the cross-cultural difference persists *unchanged*, the mechanism is not perspective-taking. Confidence would drop to 0.35.

- **East Asian speakers show systematic de dicto preference in non-reference scope tasks.** If, in independent scope-interpretation batteries measuring de re/de dicto preference outside reference contexts (e.g., "Every student saw a different painting; did every student see the *same* painting?"), East Asian speakers consistently prefer de dicto readings while Western speakers prefer de re, this resurrects the Disruptor's linguistic-scope mechanism. It would suggest the effect is rooted in language-specific scope defaults rather than reference-specific pragmatic perspective-taking. Confidence would drop to 0.25.

- **Pragmatic symmetry is not a necessary trigger.** If reformulating the Gödel case to remove ambiguity (by marking one perspective as objectively canonical) does *not* reduce the cross-cultural effect, then pragmatic symmetry is not the operative feature. The effect might instead reflect folk-theory commitment, translation artifacts, or cultural response bias independent of the case structure. Confidence would drop to 0.40.

- **Culture predicts Gödel responses independent of individual perspective-taking tendency.** If measuring individual participants' habitual perspective-taking bias (via narrative-interpretation or scene-description tasks) does *not* predict their Gödel responses, and culture remains the primary predictor even after controlling for individual perspective-taking tendency, the mechanism is not pragmatic-cognitive strategy but something more stable and group-level (folk-theory, conceptual worldview, response style bias). Confidence would drop to 0.45.

- **The effect replicates identically across native-language and translated versions with no translation-structure interaction.** If the effect is equally strong in carefully back-translated native-language versions as in English translations, translation artifacts are ruled out. However, if the effect is *weaker or reversed* in native-language versions, this suggests the mechanism may involve interaction between English grammatical structure and the way the question was originally posed, falsifying the pure pragmatic-cognitive account. Confidence would drop to 0.50.

## Directions for further research

- **Priority 1: Causal manipulation of perspective via explicit framing.** Present the Gödel case in conditions where the narrator's vs. protagonist's perspective is made explicit and marked as the *intended* perspective for evaluation. Measure whether East-West differences attenuate when both groups are primed toward the same perspective. This test directly targets the perspective-taking mechanism and would provide the single most diagnostic evidence.

- **Priority 2: Scope-interpretation battery independent of reference.** Administer non-reference scope tasks (doubly-quantified sentences, perceptual reports with ambiguous scope) to the same participant pool taking the Gödel case. Measure correlation between individual differences in de re/de dicto preference and Gödel descriptivism responses. If correlation is near-zero, linguistic scope-interpretation is ruled out; if strong, it is resurrected.

- **Priority 3: Systematic manipulation of pragmatic symmetry.** Create three versions of the Gödel case: fully symmetric (standard); with narrator's perspective marked as canonical (e.g., "Historians universally agree..."); with protagonist's perspective marked as canonical (e.g., "John, an expert, personally verified..."). Measure cross-cultural differences in each condition. Predicted outcome: largest effect in symmetric condition; attenuated effect when either perspective is marked canonical.

- **Priority 4: Within-culture individual differences in perspective-taking.** Measure perspective-taking tendency on independent tasks (narrative comprehension, relational vs. object-focused attention, theory-of-mind). Regress Gödel responses on both culture and individual perspective-taking tendency, with interaction terms. Does perspective-taking tendency mediate the cultural effect? Does it predict individual variation within cultures?

- **Priority 5: Native-language replication in Mandarin Chinese, Japanese, and Korean with rigorous translation validation.** Conduct parallel studies in each language group using carefully back-translated stimuli. Measure (a) whether effects replicate; (b) whether effect size is comparable to English versions; (c) whether any language-specific grammatical feature (e.g., classifier systems, aspect marking) modulates the effect. Include Cantonese as a control for topic-prominence while varying other features.

- **Priority 6: Mechanistic testing of epistemic perspective default.** Use eye-tracking or think-aloud protocols during Gödel case judgments to measure whether East Asian participants spontaneously adopt John's viewpoint while making their judgment, while Western participants spontaneously adopt the narrator's viewpoint. This would provide direct cognitive-processing evidence for the perspective-taking mechanism.

Linguistic structure is necessary but not sufficient; it does not *force* the interpretation.
