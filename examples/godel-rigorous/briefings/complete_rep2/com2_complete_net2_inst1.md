# Institute Briefing — Cross-Cultural Reference and the Gödel Case

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in referential intuitions (East Asians more descriptivist, Westerners more causal-historical) attach specifically to the Gödel case and not equally to other reference probes like Jonah, Twin Earth, or Peano?

## Conclusion

The Gödel effect—a ~25-point cross-cultural gap in semantic reference judgments—is empirically robust and replicates across multiple independent studies with varying methodologies and populations. However, the mechanism driving this case-specificity remains genuinely underdetermined. The effect is real, but it is a symptom of multiple interacting factors (linguistic pragmatics, case-specific structural features, and possibly cognitive-stylistic differences) rather than a pure exemplar of any single established causal mechanism. The current evidence cannot distinguish among competing hypotheses about why Gödel uniquely elicits the effect while Jonah (~13-point gap) and other cases show minimal or reversed effects.

## Confidence

**0.62**

High confidence (0.85) in the robustness of the phenomenon itself: the effect replicates across studies (Machery et al. 2004; Beebe & Undercoffer 2015, 2016; Sytsma et al. 2014), persists when confounds like epistemic perspective ambiguity are controlled (Sytsma & Livengood 2011), and appears early in development (cross-culturally at age seven). Moderate-to-low confidence (0.45) in any current proposed mechanism: three distinct causal theories are in circulation, none has been directly tested against its own falsification conditions, and the field has not constructed the critical comparative experiments needed to adjudicate among them. Moderate confidence (0.58) in case-specificity as a stable phenomenon: the gap is substantially smaller on Jonah and absent or reversed on other cases, but whether this reflects fundamentally different mechanisms on different cases or a single mechanism expressed at different intensities remains unclear.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **The Gödel effect is replicable:** Machery et al.'s original finding (2004) of a 25-point cross-cultural gap (56.5% of Western vs. 31.5% of Chinese participants endorsing causal-historical reference) replicates in Beebe & Undercoffer (2015, 2016), who tested the same case with clarified narrator's perspective and obtained consistent 13-point gaps. The 2025 large-scale study in *Philosophical Studies* (2000+ Mandarin speakers across five replication studies) reports that "the Gödel case peculiarly stands out in its ability to elicit robust cultural differences," confirming robustness across diverse samples.

- **The effect persists under methodological refinement:** Sytsma & Livengood (2011) identified epistemic perspective ambiguity in the original vignettes and posed the question from the narrator's direct perspective rather than the experimental third-person frame. The cross-cultural gap persisted even with this clarification, suggesting the effect is not an artifact of question-framing ambiguity. Subsequent clarification experiments (Machery 2015) that isolated and minimized presupposition ambiguity left the effect intact.

- **Case-specificity is empirically real but unequal:** The same cross-cultural pattern (East Asians more descriptivist, Westerners more causal-historical) appears on Jonah (~13-point gap, roughly half the Gödel effect size) and is absent, minimal, or reversed on Twin Earth, Peano, and other reference probes. This is not noise; it is a systematic pattern documented in large-N studies designed specifically to test case-sensitivity.

- **Three competing mechanisms have been proposed but not directly tested:**
  1. *Linguistic pragmatics (bare nominals + presupposition systems):* Mandarin lacks articles and grammaticized definiteness, and uses classifiers that may embed presupposition ambiguity. Prediction: effect should disappear in English translation; should generalize to other cases with symmetric bare-nominal structure.
  2. *Folk-theory divergence (culture-independent of language):* East Asian holistic/relational cognition (Nisbett et al.) shapes how speakers conceptualize identity and persistence, independent of linguistic form. Prediction: effect should generalize equally across all cases; should persist in bilinguals tested in English.
  3. *Interaction of bare nominals + case structure + question pragmatics:* Gödel is uniquely stripped of narrative scaffolding, external knowledge anchors, or canonical historical referents, which combines with presupposition ambiguity to create maximum pragmatic symmetry. Prediction: effect should disappear when one description is marked as historically canonical; should generalize to other pragmatically-symmetric cases.

- **No mechanism has been falsified, but none has been confirmed:** Each theory makes testable predictions. None has been directly tested. The field has verified that perspective clarification (Sytsma & Livengood) does not eliminate the effect, suggesting question-framing artifacts are not the culprit. But language-shift experiments, pragmatic-symmetry manipulations, and parallel-case constructions—the critical tests—remain unexecuted.

- **Experimental philosophy replication rates are higher than general psychology:** The cited meta-analysis (22% interaction-effect replication in psychology) does not apply to the Gödel case's actual statistical structure. The Experimental Philosophy Replication Project reports median effect-size relative to original of 0.86 and success rates around 70%, substantially above psychology's baseline. The Gödel effect falls squarely in this higher-replicating domain.

- **Stimulus bias has been attenuated but not ruled out:** Machery et al.'s original vignettes were susceptible to criticism about perspective, presupposition, and narrative framing. These confounds have been systematically tested and controlled in subsequent experiments (Beebe & Undercoffer, Sytsma et al., Machery 2015). The effect survives these controls. However, experimenters' theoretical priors may still influence stimulus design in ways that favor expected outcomes; this cannot be assessed from published sources alone.

## What would falsify this

- **Large-scale preregistered replication failure:** If a high-powered, preregistered replication study (N > 1000) with explicit sample-composition documentation and rigorous translation controls fails to detect the cross-cultural gap on Gödel, the phenomenon's robustness collapses. Current evidence does not predict this, but it remains the sharpest empirical test.

- **Pragmatic-canonicality effect:** If the Gödel case is reformulated so that one description is socially or historically canonicalized (e.g., "Historians unanimously establish that Gödel discovered incompleteness; one person falsely claimed credit") and the cross-cultural gap vanishes, pragmatic symmetry is the operative feature. This would strongly support the linguistic-pragmatics mechanism and falsify folk-theory accounts that predict equal effects regardless of canonicality.

- **Failure of pragmatic-symmetry generalization:** If constructed parallel cases with matched pragmatic symmetry but entirely different content (e.g., medical discovery, artistic attribution) show no cross-cultural effect, then Gödel-specificity reflects idiosyncratic features of Gödel (its intellectual domain, historical familiarity, logical complexity, narrative structure) rather than a general sensitivity to pragmatic ambiguity. This would falsify the pragmatic-symmetry hypothesis.

- **Language-shift null result:** If bilingual speakers tested in both Mandarin and English show identical cross-cultural gaps in both conditions, this falsifies the hypothesis that bare-nominal presupposition systems (without articles, without grammaticized definiteness) drive the effect. If the gap disappears in English translation, it supports linguistic mechanisms.

- **Processing-time evidence of non-pragmatic factors:** If eye-tracking or response-time studies reveal that East Asian speakers form referential hypotheses as quickly and as deterministically as Western speakers, this undermines accounts that posit cautious, deferred, or holistic processing. It would suggest that the difference is conceptual, not pragmatic-processing-based.

- **Demographic or translation confound:** If the effect disappears when translations are validated by independent bilingual speakers or when demographic sampling is restricted to speakers with identical educational background and philosophical exposure, the effect may reflect translation artifacts or confounded group differences rather than semantic divergence.

## Directions for further research

1. **Direct mechanism testing via language-shift design:** Test bilingual Mandarin-English speakers on the Gödel case in both native languages with identical vignette structure and reference question. If the cross-cultural gap persists in English translation, linguistic pragmatics (bare nominals, presupposition) is not the sole driver. If it disappears, this strongly supports Institute 0's mechanism. *This is the highest-priority experiment.*

2. **Pragmatic-symmetry manipulation:** Construct three versions of the Gödel case: (a) original (equal descriptions, no canonical anchor); (b) one description marked as historically canonical ("Historians universally credit Gödel..."); (c) both descriptions canonicalized via independent scholarly sources. Test on Western and East Asian speakers in native languages. Prediction: if pragmatic symmetry is operative, the cross-cultural gap should vanish or shrink in versions (b) and (c). If folk-theory differences drive the effect, the gap should persist equally.

3. **Parallel-case construction:** Design three thought experiments structurally parallel to Gödel (intellectual discovery, two competing descriptions, maximal pragmatic symmetry) but with entirely different domains (e.g., medical pathology discovery, archaeological artifact attribution, mathematical theorem priority). Test on same populations. Prediction: if pragmatic symmetry is the operative feature, cross-cultural gaps should generalize. If Gödel is idiosyncratic, gaps should be absent or much smaller in new cases.

4. **Response-time and eye-tracking study:** Test speakers' speed of referential commitment, pattern of information integration, and eye-fixation patterns (on descriptions vs. narrative context) during processing of the Gödel vignette. Prediction: if East Asian responses reflect pragmatic caution, processing times should be longer and eye patterns should show more attention to the full field of description. If responses reflect conceptual differences, processing patterns should not differ substantially.

5. **Explicit falsification test of folk-theory accounts:** Test whether other cases commonly associated with holistic/analytic cognitive differences (e.g., categorization of objects varying in family-resemblance structure, or scenarios with competing relational vs. feature-based descriptions) show parallel cross-cultural gaps. Prediction: if folk-theory differences are general, gaps should appear across domains. If Gödel is special, gaps should be absent elsewhere.

6. **Publication and selection bias audit:** Conduct a systematic review of all attempted reference-semantics studies in experimental philosophy that tested cross-cultural differences. Map the distribution of null, weak, and strong effects across cases and populations. Identify whether studies with null results on Gödel are underrepresented in the literature. This cannot replace a preregistered replication, but it can quantify the extent of publication bias.

7. **Convergent evidence from independent methods:** Replicate the Gödel effect using alternative paradigms (forced-choice vs. free-response vs. rating-scale judgments; explicit philosophical instruction vs. naive subjects; visual vs. linguistic presentation of scenarios) to confirm that the effect is robust to measurement methodology and not an artifact of the forced-binary-choice structure.

## Open disagreements in the cell

The three institutes agree that the Gödel effect is empirically real and robust. They disagree fundamentally on mechanism:

- **Institute 0 (Linguistic Pragmatism)** argues that Mandarin's bare-nominal presupposition systems (absence of articles, use of classifiers) interact with the pragmatically symmetric description structure of Gödel to make East Asian speakers defer to the explicit description given, while Western speakers default to causal-historical semantics. Institute 0 predicts the effect should disappear in English translation and should generalize to other pragmatically-symmetric cases.

- **Institute 2 (Folk-Theory Divergence)** argues that East Asian holistic and relational cognitive styles (Nisbett's analytic/dialectical framework) shape how speakers conceptualize identity and persistence independently of linguistic form. Institute 2 predicts the effect should generalize equally across all cases and should persist in bilinguals tested in English.

- **Institute 3 (Structural Interaction)** argues that the effect arises from the specific interaction of bare nominals + case structure (intellectual domain, lack of narrative scaffolding, absence of external knowledge anchors) + pragmatic symmetry in the question. Institute 3 predicts the effect should disappear when descriptions are canonicalized and should generalize to other pragmatically-symmetric cases but not to cases with narrative anchoring or knowledge accessibility.

**The Disruptor's critique** identifies the strongest gap: no institute has constructed the comparative experiments needed to test its own falsification conditions. All three accounts are internally consistent with the current evidence. The cell recommends treating this as a call for the next research phase, not as evidence that the phenomenon itself is artifact or that current theories are equally likely to be correct.
