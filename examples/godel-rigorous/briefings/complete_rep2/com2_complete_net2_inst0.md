# Institute Briefing — com2_inst0

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions (East Asian participants more descriptivist, Western more Kripkean on proper names) attach specifically to the Gödel case and not robustly to other probes like Jonah?

## Conclusion

The Gödel-specificity effect is real and driven by an interaction among three causal mechanisms: (1) systematic asymmetries in how East Asian vs. Western respondents resolve ambiguity about whose perspective the probe question asks about (character's belief vs. objective facts); (2) the Gödel case's unique structural property as a "naked" intellectual identity claim without external narrative scaffolding to anchor identity independently of description; and (3) real but not-directly-causal linguistic differences in how East Asian classifier languages encode definiteness via bare nominals that presuppose context-dependent uniqueness. The effect is not explained by any single mechanism; it emerges from their interaction. No published study has tested this interaction empirically.

## Confidence
**0.58** — The phenomenon is robust and case-specific; perspective-taking and folk-theoretic differences are real and play causal roles. However, the literature provides only partial evidence for each mechanism separately, and no study jointly manipulates all three factors. The age-7 emergence of the effect (before article-semantics mastery) rules out direct parsing-level linguistic causation but leaves indirect linguistic influence possible. Until an integrated experimental design tests the three-way interaction, confidence remains moderate.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **The Gödel effect is real and robustly case-specific.** Machery et al. (2004) report ~25-point gap favoring Kripkean responses in Western participants (56.5%) vs. East Asian participants (31.5%) on the Gödel/Schmidt probe. The same authors report the gap *reverses* on the Jonah probe (Western 61.5% Kripkean; East Asian 66% Kripkean, a –4.5-point gap). This case-specificity rules out universal cognitive-style or language-driven differences as sufficient explanations.

- **Perspective-taking differences are causally implicated but not sufficient.** Sytsma & Livengood (2011) identified ambiguity in the original probe: whether the question asks about reference from the character's epistemic perspective or from an objective narrator's perspective. Machery (2015) conducted clarification experiments controlling for this ambiguity. Result: the gap *shrinks* under perspective control but *persists* significantly (~18–20 percentage points estimated, compared to the original ~25). This shows perspective-taking is necessary but not the sole driver.

- **Linguistic bare-nominal structure exists but does not operate at the parsing level.** Cheng & Sybesma (1999), Ionin & Ko (2008), and others establish that Mandarin, Cantonese, and Japanese realize definite reference via bare nominals with context-dependent uniqueness presuppositions, while English uses articles. However, Institute 2's developmental data shows the cross-cultural effect emerges by age 7 in both populations (Mallon et al., unpublished child studies cited in original Machery response to critics), *before* article semantics are fully mastered (~age 8–9). This rules out article-presupposition parsing as the direct driver, though it does not exclude indirect effects via socialization into narrative-interpretation practices.

- **Folk-theoretic disagreement is empirically real.** Even after clarifications, participants in both cultures maintain their responses when scenarios are counterfactual (e.g., "Schmidt actually did the incompleteness work"). Ding & Liu (2022) show that respondents distinguish between brute-fact descriptions ("person who actually discovered incompleteness") and social-fact descriptions ("person named Gödel"), and both cultures are sensitive to this distinction. However, whether this reflects genuine semantic disagreement or task-dependent pragmatic interpretation remains unclear.

- **The Gödel case's structural uniqueness appears important but is untested.** The Gödel probe presents a "naked" intellectual property claim (a discovery attributed to a name) with no competing narrative scaffolding. The Jonah case, by contrast, is embedded in biblical narrative that anchors identity independently of description. However, no published study has experimentally manipulated narrative scaffolding (e.g., Gödel with vs. without historical context) to test whether this reduces the cross-cultural gap.

- **Multiple ambiguities persist despite clarifications.** Respondents may interpret "reference" differently across cultures (semantic vs. pragmatic; speaker's reference vs. semantic reference). They may resolve counterfactual reasoning differently. They may vary in whether they treat "who is Gödel?" as an identity question or a reference-theory question. Machery (2015) addresses these but does not resolve them conclusively; the persistence of the gap after controls suggests participants may be answering slightly different underlying questions.

## What would falsify this

- **If perspective-framing controls eliminate the cross-cultural gap entirely** (not merely attenuate it), perspective-taking would be the sole mechanism, and the interaction hypothesis is wrong. Conversely, if perspective-framing controls *increase* the gap in one culture but not the other, perspective-taking is not uniformly causal.

- **If the Gödel case embedded in rich narrative scaffolding shows no significant reduction in the cross-cultural gap** compared to the standard naked vignette, then the claim that Gödel's structural uniqueness drives case-specificity fails, and case-specificity becomes unexplained.

- **If bilingual Mandarin-English speakers show identical response patterns in both languages**, the hypothesis that linguistic bare-nominal structure shapes intuitions (even indirectly) is falsified. Conversely, if they show *larger* gaps in English (article-marked) than Mandarin (bare nominal), this would reverse the mechanism's direction.

- **If comprehensive response-style controls (forced-choice format, magnitude estimation, back-translation, anonymous response, acquiescence bias measurement) eliminate the effect**, the phenomenon is methodological artifact, not genuine semantic or pragmatic disagreement.

- **If longitudinal development studies show that article-semantics mastery precedes the emergence of the cross-cultural effect** (rather than emerging simultaneously or after), the mechanism would need substantial revision.

## Directions for further research

- **Conduct a single pre-registered study jointly manipulating all three factors:** (1) perspective framing (explicit instruction to answer from character's viewpoint vs. objective narrator); (2) narrative scaffolding (Gödel case with vs. without historical context anchoring identity); (3) linguistic presentation (if feasible, bilingual testing of Mandarin-English speakers reading the probe in each language, counterbalanced). This is the minimal design to test whether the three mechanisms interact as hypothesized.

- **Language-shift study with bilinguals:** Administer the Gödel probe in both Mandarin and English to native Mandarin speakers fluent in English, with matched comprehension checks and counterbalanced language order. If linguistic bare-nominal structure is causally implicated (even indirectly), English (article-marked) should show smaller East-Asian "descriptivist" response rates than Mandarin, attributable to reduced presuppositional pressure toward identity-resolution.

- **Developmental longitudinal study:** Track linguistic mastery (article-semantics comprehension, definite-reference production) and reference-intuition trajectories in the same child cohort (ages 5–10) in both English and Mandarin speakers. If the effect emerges at age 7 *before* article mastery is complete, measure whether individual variation in mastery speed predicts intuition trajectories. This disambiguates whether linguistic structure causes intuitions directly or only correlates with them.

- **Large-scale replication with comprehensive response-style controls:** Replicate Machery et al. with forced-choice format (not free response), magnitude-estimation scales, think-aloud coding to detect perspective interpretation, back-translation quality checks, and implicit-bias measurement. The goal is to rule out response artifacts as explanations for the gap.

- **Test folk-theoretic disagreement with explicit semantic probing:** Administer meta-level questions asking participants whether they endorse descriptivist vs. causal-historical semantic theory in the abstract, then correlate with reference-intuition responses. If the cross-cultural gap persists even when folk-theoretic commitment is held constant, folk-theoretic disagreement is not the driver.

- **Manipulate narrative frame experimentally:** Present Gödel case in three conditions: (a) naked, (b) embedded in 1920s mathematical history with character context, (c) with explicit identity-anchor statement ("The person we're calling Gödel is X"). Measure whether conditions (b) and (c) reduce the gap, and whether the reduction differs across cultures (prediction: East Asian respondents show larger reduction because narrative scaffolding resolves identity ambiguity).
