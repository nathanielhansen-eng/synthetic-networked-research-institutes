# Institute Briefing — com2_inst0

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions (East Asian vs. Western participants favoring descriptivist responses) appear robustly only in the Gödel case and not in other reference probes like Jonah, Twin Earth, or Peano?

## Conclusion

The Gödel effect is not a general difference in how East Asians and Westerners conceptualize reference or categorize objects. It is a *case-specific* response pattern triggered by a feature unique to the Gödel case: both candidate descriptions (the "real discoverer" and the "person who stole the proof") are pragmatically *symmetric*—neither is marked as socially canonical in the way that the biblical Jonah or discovered water are in their respective cases. East Asian respondents, whose languages rely heavily on context and presupposition rather than mandatory definiteness marking, appear to adopt a *pragmatic conservatism heuristic* when faced with such ambiguous descriptions, defaulting to the explicitly-stated description rather than inferring which entity a speaker intends to single out. The mechanism is linguistic-pragmatic, not cognitive-stylistic.

## Confidence

**0.58** — The claim is tightly constrained and makes testable predictions, and it integrates established negative findings (question-ambiguity alone is insufficient; Nisbett's general analytic/holistic framework does not explain case-specificity). However, the specific mechanism has not yet been directly tested in the literature, and multiple pathways could produce the observed pattern.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **The Gödel specificity is empirically robust.** Machery, Mallon, Nichols & Stich (2004) reported cross-cultural differences in the Gödel case. Recent large-scale replication studies sampling over 2,000 Mandarin Chinese speakers confirm that the effect attaches specifically to the Gödel case and is weak, absent, or reversed for Jonah, Twin Earth, and Peano cases (Sytsma & Livengood replication; 2025 large-scale study). This is the foundation for the puzzle.

- **Simple question-ambiguity cannot explain the effect.** Sytsma & Livengood demonstrated that the original Machery et al. probe was ambiguous between speaker's reference (what John intends to refer to) and semantic reference (what the name literally refers to). However, subsequent studies that explicitly disambiguated the question—presenting presuppositions "in an easy to follow manner"—found that the cross-cultural gap *persisted*. This directly falsifies the hypothesis that question-ambiguity alone drives the effect. Both Western and East Asian respondents are affected by ambiguity, yet the cross-cultural difference survives disambiguation.

- **Nisbett's holistic/analytic cognitive framework is real but insufficient.** Pioneering work by Masuda and Nisbett demonstrates that Westerners display analytic attentional bias (focus on salient objects) while East Asians display holistic bias (attention to entire field and relationships). This framework is well-established. However, it does not explain *why* the Gödel case specifically triggers the effect when other reference cases do not—a general cognitive difference should generalize across cases, yet it does not.

- **The Gödel case has unique pragmatic structure.** Unlike Jonah (where the biblical story is the canonical reference) or Twin Earth (where water/XYZ has a clear discovery narrative), the Gödel case presents two descriptions of *equal pragmatic salience* with neither marked as socially canonical. The "real discoverer" and "the person who stole the proof" are both vivid, available, and plausible—the scenario does not privilege one as "the" entity in the way other cases do.

- **East Asian languages handle definiteness through pragmatic systems, not mandatory grammatical marking.** Mandarin and Cantonese use numeral classifiers (e.g., "Cl+N") to mark definiteness, but rely heavily on presupposition maximization and context rather than overt articles. Countable bare nouns in classifier languages freely occur in argument position for unique definite use. This pragmatic system may produce a different default heuristic: when neither description is explicitly canonicalized and the question is ambiguous, speakers may *conservatively* stick to the explicitly-stated description rather than inferring which entity the speaker intends.

- **Response-conservatism is a documented phenomenon in psycholinguistics.** When speakers face ambiguous or underspecified reference, different populations show different strategies for resolving it. East Asian pragmatic systems, which maximize use of context rather than explicit marking, may cultivate a bias toward pragmatic caution when context itself is absent or ambiguous.

## What would falsify this

- **If the Gödel effect disappears when one description is explicitly marked as socially or epistemically canonical.** For example, if the probe states "Historians universally accept that Gödel discovered incompleteness, though some rival claims exist" before asking about reference, and the cross-cultural effect vanishes, then pragmatic symmetry is not the operative feature.

- **If East Asian speakers show the Gödel-like effect (descriptivist bias) on *other deliberately constructed cases* where both descriptions are equally unmarked as canonical,** the effect would generalize beyond Gödel and confirm the pragmatic-symmetry mechanism. Conversely, if no other case shows the effect despite pragmatic symmetry, the Gödel case may have additional idiosyncratic features.

- **If speakers of classifier languages (Mandarin, Japanese, Korean) tested in their native language show *weaker* or *equal* cross-cultural differences compared to speakers of mandatory-article languages (English, French, German),** the linguistic-pragmatic mechanism would be weakened. If effects are language-independent, the mechanism is epiphenomenal.

- **If eye-tracking or response-time data show that East Asian respondents are *equally fast* as Western respondents in forming a referential hypothesis on the Gödel case, but choose differently,** this would argue against the pragmatic-caution mechanism and suggest instead a deliberate (perhaps metacognitive) inference about which answer counts as "correct."

## Directions for further research

- **Direct test of pragmatic symmetry.** Construct parallel versions of reference cases where both descriptions are explicitly canonicalized (Gödel-Plus: "Historians agree Gödel did it, but some claim Schmidt did"). Test both East Asian and Western populations. If the cross-cultural effect collapses, pragmatic symmetry is the operative feature.

- **Cross-linguistic replication in native languages.** Administer the original Gödel probe and parallel cases to Mandarin, Japanese, Korean, and English speakers in their native languages, with explicit attention to how definiteness is marked in each translation. A single-language study cannot adjudicate whether the effect is linguistic or merely cultural. Comparison across classifier vs. article languages is essential.

- **Pragmatic-conservatism task battery.** Develop a battery of tasks that measure respondents' tendency toward pragmatic conservatism when reference is ambiguous and no description is privileged (e.g., forced-choice tasks with ambiguous pronouns, definite descriptions with multiple possible referents). If East Asian respondents are generally more conservative, the mechanism gains support.

- **Meta-analysis of all published Gödel-case studies.** Systematic review of which specific versions of the probe show cross-cultural effects and which do not. Map which question-phrasings, translation choices, and case-orderings preserve or eliminate the effect. This will reveal whether the effect is robust to variation or fragile.

- **Psycholinguistic processing study.** Eye-tracking and response-time measurement while participants read the Gödel scenario and answer the probe. Does the East Asian group show longer latencies before committing to an answer? Do they fixate differently on the two descriptions? Processing data could distinguish between pragmatic caution and other mechanisms.

- **Extension to other canonical asymmetry cases.** Test reference intuitions on cases where descriptions *are* pragmatically symmetric (e.g., an artificial case where two equally-discovered aspects of a phenomenon are equally salient and neither has a historical canonical status). If the cross-cultural effect replicates on these constructed cases, the mechanism is confirmed.

The Disruptor's revised position and the cell's synthesis favor the linguistic-pragmatic framing, but direct evidence for this causal claim does not yet exist in the literature.
