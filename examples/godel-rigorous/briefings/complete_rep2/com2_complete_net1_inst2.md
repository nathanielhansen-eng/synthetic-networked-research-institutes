# Institute Briefing — com2_inst2

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions (East Asians more descriptivist, Westerners more causal-historical) attach specifically to the Gödel case and not equally to other probes like Jonah?

## Conclusion

The cross-cultural effect reflects divergent **folk theories of reference** (descriptivist vs. causal-historical), not a linguistic parsing artifact imposed by the absence of definite articles in Chinese. The Gödel case is structurally distinctive because it forces a direct, stark choice between these two theories by presenting competing descriptions in a counterfactual scenario, making the underlying conceptual disagreement maximally salient. The effect persists across multiple probes, emerges by age 7 before article semantics are fully developed, and survives disambiguating clarifications—all findings inconsistent with a linguistic mechanism operating at the level of parsing.

## Confidence
**0.72** — Multiple independent falsification tests support the folk-theory account, but the literature has not yet directly tested the strongest diagnostic: whether removing competing descriptions from the probe shifts East Asian responses toward causal-historical reasoning. Indirect evidence from linguistic typology (topic-prominence in Chinese, article systems in English) suggests language *shapes* conceptual orientation, but the mechanism remains empirically underdetermined.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **The effect does not disappear when ambiguity is controlled.** Machery (2015) and Machery et al. (2013) clarified the Gödel probe to minimize speaker's reference/semantic reference ambiguity. The cross-cultural difference persisted. If the mechanism were purely linguistic (article-less languages forcing description-reliance), removing ambiguity—which would reduce pressure to use descriptions for disambiguation—should weaken the effect. It does not. *Falsifies the direct linguistic mechanism.*

- **The effect emerges by age 7, before article semantics are mastered.** Sytsma et al. (2018) documented systematic cross-cultural variation between Chinese and English speakers in a truth-value judgment task, with such differences evident by age 7 in both populations. This timing is too early for article-mastery differences to be the locus, ruling out a parsing-based mechanism. *Strongly falsifies the linguistic hypothesis.*

- **The effect generalizes across probes, not confined to Gödel.** Machery et al. (2004, 2006) and subsequent work by Machery (2015) document the cross-cultural pattern in modified Gödel cases and Jonah-like cases. While effect sizes vary, the effect is not uniquely tied to the Gödel case's linguistic complexity. *Weakens the claim that linguistic structure determines specificity.*

- **Descriptive ambiguity persists across all groups.** Ding & Liu (2022) show that participants (East Asian and Western alike) associate different types of descriptions with proper names—brute-fact descriptions (e.g., "discovered incompleteness") vs. social-fact descriptions (e.g., "called by this name"). Both groups are sensitive to this distinction, but it does not eliminate the cross-cultural difference. This suggests the locus is not *reliance on descriptions per se*, but rather *which theory of how names refer to individuals* participants endorse.

- **The candidate alternative explanation fits the data.** Machery (2015) argues that the effect reflects culturally variable cognitive styles: Western analytic cognition privileges causal chains and rigid designation, while East Asian holistic cognition privileges thematic coherence and property-based identification. This is orthogonal to the linguistic mechanism and accommodates the robustness and generalization of the effect across probes and ages. However, this explanation remains a *candidate* rather than empirically vindicated.

## What would falsify this

- **Direct test of description-dependence.** Reformulate the Gödel probe to eliminate competing descriptions entirely, replacing "the person who discovered" vs. "the person who stole" with a pure causal-chain frame (e.g., "John learned the name 'Gödel' from person X via a historical chain. X is actually Schmidt. Does 'Gödel' refer to Gödel or Schmidt?"). If East Asian participants shift significantly toward causal-historical responses, the effect is description-dependent, not folk-theory-dependent. If they remain descriptivist, the folk-theory account is strengthened.

- **Response-style confounds.** If a comprehensive replication controlling for acquiescence bias, social desirability, and translation artifacts reveals that the effect disappears or reverses when these confounds are removed, the phenomenon may not reflect genuine conceptual disagreement but methodological artifact.

- **Failure of generalization.** If the effect is found only in the Gödel case and consistently absent in other probes once effect sizes are properly compared across studies, the claim that it reflects a general folk-theory difference would be undermined.

## Directions for further research

- **Reformulate and retest:** Conduct a preregistered study presenting East Asian and Western participants with three versions of the probe: (1) the standard Gödel case with competing descriptions; (2) a version removing descriptions in favor of pure causal-chain framing; (3) a hybrid. Measure both response proportions and confidence/certainty. This directly tests whether the effect is description-dependent or folk-theory-based.

- **Disentangle linguistic typology from conceptual orientation via language acquisition:** Conduct a longitudinal or cross-generational study of Chinese immigrants to English-speaking countries. Do their folk-theory intuitions about the Gödel case shift as they acquire English and internalize article semantics? If yes, this supports indirect linguistic influence on conceptual orientation. If no, language structure is epiphenomenal.

- **Control for response style systematically:** Replicate Machery et al. (2004) with explicit controls for acquiescence bias (e.g., counterbalanced forced-choice formats, magnitude estimation tasks, open-ended explanations coded by blind raters). If the effect persists after these controls, confidence in the phenomenon rises.

- **Test intermediate cases:** Design probes that vary the *salience and complexity* of competing descriptions without removing them entirely (e.g., Gödel case vs. a case where the counterfactual is less sharp, vs. a case where the descriptions are more aligned). This tests whether the Gödel case's distinctiveness is about description-density, logical complexity, or conceptual clarity.

- **Age-graded acquisitional study:** Test Chinese and English speakers at ages 5, 7, 9, 12, and 16. If the effect is linguistic, it should correlate with article-mastery milestones. If it is conceptual, it should remain stable or follow cultural socialization patterns (e.g., strengthening in adolescence as conceptual frameworks mature).

## Open disagreements in the cell

The Disruptor and Architect converge on the rejection of the direct linguistic mechanism (articles → parsing → description-reliance) but disagree on the residual role of language:

- **The Disruptor** argues that linguistic typology (Chinese topic-prominence, English article systems) indirectly shapes conceptual orientations over development. Language does not force parsing in the moment, but over time it socializes speakers into different default theories of reference. This is plausible but remains empirically underdetermined.

- **The Architect** emphasizes that the falsification tests rule out the direct linguistic mechanism and that the effect is best explained by cultural-cognitive differences in analytic vs. holistic reasoning (Nisbett et al.). The Architect does not deny that language plays a role but insists the mechanism must be demonstrated empirically, not inferred.

**The unresolved question:** Is the folk-theory difference a *consequence* of linguistic typology (language shapes thought over time) or *independent* of it (culture and cognition are the primary drivers, and language is a symptom rather than a cause)? The evidence cannot yet distinguish these, and both accounts predict the same empirical outcomes. Resolving this requires intervention—either language acquisition studies or comparison of non-native speakers—that the current literature does not provide.
