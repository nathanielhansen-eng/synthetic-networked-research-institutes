# Institute Briefing — com1_inst0

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions (East Asian descriptivist vs. Western Kripkean) attach *specifically* to the Gödel case and not robustly to other probes like Jonah or Twin Earth?

## Conclusion

The Gödel case peculiarly elicits robust cross-cultural differences (Western participants more Kripkean, East Asian participants more descriptivist) that do not appear with equal strength on other reference probes. The mechanism is most likely a **convergence of linguistic pragmatics and perspective-taking ambiguity**, not counterfactual reasoning avoidance as initially hypothesized. Bare noun phrases in Chinese and Japanese create parsing ambiguity when resolving identity under counterfactual conditions, *and* the Gödel case contains an indexical perspective ambiguity (whose description? narrator or community?) that East Asian participants resolve toward actual-world anchoring. No single mechanism dominates; the case-specificity emerges from a structural feature of *this particular probe* that other Kripkean cases lack.

## Confidence
**0.55** — The core empirical finding (case-specificity to Gödel) is robust and replicates across languages and populations. However, the *mechanism* remains genuinely underdetermined because the three leading hypotheses (counterfactual avoidance, perspective-taking ambiguity, bare-noun pragmatics) each have empirical support and each face critical un-tested predictions. The confidence is deliberately low because falsifying any one mechanism requires direct interventions that do not yet exist in the literature.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **Case-specificity is real.** Machery et al. (2004) and subsequent large-scale replications (including studies sampling 2000+ Mandarin Chinese speakers) confirm that the Gödel case elicits robust cross-cultural differences, while Jonah and Twin Earth cases show weaker, absent, or reversed effects. This is the anchor empirical fact.

- **Jonah case disrupts the counterfactual avoidance account.** Machery et al. reported cross-cultural differences in responses to cases patterned after Kripke's Jonah case, with Western participants significantly more likely to choose the Kripkean answer. Since the Jonah case does not force a counterfactual identity shift in the way Gödel does (it is simply loosely associated with multiple descriptions), the hypothesis that *counterfactual reasoning avoidance specifically explains Gödel* is damaged. A global "East Asian avoidance of counterfactual reasoning" would predict equal or larger effects on Jonah; the data show weaker effects, suggesting case-specificity tracks something other than counterfactual burden alone.

- **Developmental timing argues against mature counterfactual reasoning as primary mechanism.** Cross-cultural differences on Gödel are already in place by age 7. Counterfactual reasoning competence and mature reasoning about identity under counterfactuals develop later (ages 8–10+). Early emergence suggests the mechanism is more foundational—linguistic scaffolding or pragmatic-inferential habit—rather than a cognitive style that consolidates with mature reasoning.

- **Native-language persistence rules out translation/response-style confounds.** Machery et al. (2010) replicated the core Gödel effect when presenting the probe to Hong Kong participants in Mandarin Chinese rather than English. Izumi et al. (2018) identified a confound: bare noun phrases (articleless) in Chinese and Japanese create multiple interpretations absent from English definite descriptions. However, the persistence of the effect even in native language, and with clarified presuppositions, shows that translation ambiguity and response-style bias alone do not account for the pattern. Something structural about the *content* or framing of the Gödel case drives the cross-cultural difference.

- **Linguistic pragmatics of bare nouns is plausible but untested.** Bare noun phrases in Mandarin and Japanese are multiply ambiguous—they can receive weak-definite, existential, or generic interpretations depending on context (topic position, negation scope, aspect marking). When forced to resolve a counterfactual identity claim ("Is John's use of 'Gödel' really about Schmidt?"), East Asian participants may pragmatically re-anchor to the description that is actually satisfied in the world rather than lock a reference chain to a counterfactually-specified individual. **Critical missing test:** Presenting the Gödel case in Mandarin using explicit demonstratives ("那個人" — "that person") rather than bare nouns should reduce this re-anchoring pressure if bare-noun ambiguity is primary. This test does not exist in the literature.

- **Perspective-taking ambiguity is also plausible.** The Gödel case invokes an indexical ambiguity: "From whose perspective is Gödel described?" The narrator's perspective (Schmidt did the work), the community's perspective (Gödel did the work), or the epistemically-privileged perspective (here's what actually happened). East Asian cognitive styles are described as more field-dependent and holistic, attending to the full context rather than isolating a single causal chain. When forced into an ambiguous perspective-taking scenario, East Asian participants may revert to what is actually true in the widest field (the community's and narrator's actual knowledge), rather than tracking a name through a counterfactual shift. **Critical missing test:** Disambiguating the Gödel case by clearly marking whose perspective is authoritative (e.g., "The scholarly consensus is that Gödel proved incompleteness, though John was misinformed. What does 'Gödel' refer to in John's speech?") should reduce the effect if perspective ambiguity is primary.

- **Counterfactual reasoning is not categorically absent in East Asian thought.** Classical and modern Chinese use counterfactual markers (e.g., 假如, 要是) and conditional expressions, and Chinese philosophers engaged in counterfactual reasoning. The cross-cultural difference is not a global avoidance of counterfactuals but a *differential tolerance* for maintaining a name-referent binding under counterfactual disruption when combined with linguistic ambiguity and perspective uncertainty.

- **The Gödel case is *uniquely* ambiguous in a way other Kripkean cases are not.** Unlike Jonah (loosely associated with multiple descriptions, but no counterfactual disruption) or Twin Earth (natural-kind extension, not individual identity), Gödel combines: (a) an identity-falsifying fact (Schmidt, not Gödel, did the work), (b) forced counterfactual reasoning ("What if the person who did the work were the one John learned about?"), and (c) a perspective ambiguity (whose description is authoritative—the actual community or John's misinformed version?). This combination is case-specific.

## What would falsify this

- **Demonstrative test in Mandarin.** If reformulating the Gödel case to use explicit demonstratives ("那個人証明了不完備性" — "that person proved incompleteness") rather than bare nouns reduces the East Asian descriptivist response by ≥15 percentage points, bare-noun pragmatics is primary. If it does not, linguistic structure is secondary to perspective-taking or response bias.

- **Perspective-disambiguation test.** If reformulating Gödel to clearly mark whose perspective is authoritative (e.g., "The true fact is that Gödel proved it. John mistakenly believes Schmidt proved it. When John says 'Gödel proved incompleteness,' what does he refer to?") reduces the East Asian descriptivist response, perspective-taking ambiguity is operative. If the effect persists at equal strength, perspective ambiguity is not the primary driver.

- **Counterfactual removal test.** If reformulating Gödel to ask "In the world as described, whom does John's community call 'Gödel'?" without forcing a counterfactual shift ("What if the person who did X were the one John learned about?") reduces the East Asian descriptivist rate by ≥15 percentage points, counterfactual burden contributes. If the effect persists at ≥65% East Asian descriptivist rate, counterfactual reasoning avoidance is not operative.

- **Jonah effect size quantification.** If the Jonah case effect size (Cohen's d) is substantially smaller than Gödel's (d < 0.3 vs. d > 0.6) in the same population, case-specificity is robust and supported. If they are equal in effect size (both d > 0.5), the mechanism is global, not counterfactual-specific, contradicting the Disruptor's account.

- **Non-verbal child task with pre-conventional populations.** If children aged 5–6 (before explicit cultural learning of naming conventions) show no cross-cultural difference on a pointing or object-allocation task modeled on Gödel, the effect is culturally learned later (age 7+). If they do show cross-cultural differences, the mechanism is foundational and cognitively primitive, suggesting linguistic rather than cultural reasoning drives it.

- **Cross-linguistic test beyond pro-drop languages.** If the effect appears with equal strength in non-pro-drop East Asian languages (e.g., Burmese, Thai, or Vietnamese), pro-drop structure is not the causal mechanism. If it disappears, syntactic licensing of bare nouns and their pragmatic interpretation is central.

## Directions for further research

- **Priority 1: Direct intervention on the three candidate mechanisms.** Run three parallel studies—one manipulating bare-noun vs. demonstrative reference in Mandarin, one disambiguating perspective-taking in English (and Mandarin), and one removing counterfactual structure—on the same population (2000+ participants, balanced East Asian and Western). This is the fastest path to falsification.

- **Priority 2: Quantify Jonah effect sizes.** Re-analyze or rerun the Machery et al. Jonah probe with explicit effect-size reporting (Cohen's d, 95% CIs) for East Asian vs. Western groups. If Jonah-d is ≤0.3 and Gödel-d is ≥0.6, the Disruptor's counterfactual hypothesis gains purchase. If they are comparable, case-specificity needs a different explanation.

- **Priority 3: Developmental trajectory study.** Run the Gödel probe (in age-appropriate form) on children aged 5, 7, 9, and 11 in both East Asian and Western populations, using identical methodology. Plot the emergence and consolidation of cross-cultural differences. If they emerge at age 5–6, the mechanism is foundational (linguistic); if at age 8+, it reflects learned cultural reasoning norms.

- **Priority 4: Test demonstrative effects in other languages.** Extend the bare-noun vs. demonstrative manipulation to Japanese, Korean, and Mandarin. If the effect is large in all three, bare-noun pragmatics is robust across pro-drop languages. If variable, syntax is less important than pragmatic context or cultural framing.

- **Priority 5: Establish whether Twin Earth and Jonah cases truly lack Gödel-sized effects.** Run high-powered studies (N > 1000 per language group) on all three cases using identical methodology. If Jonah and Twin Earth are truly non-significant or small-effect (d < 0.2), case-specificity is genuine. If they are medium-effect (d > 0.4) but *numerically smaller* than Gödel, the mechanism may be a global difference in reference intuitions modulated by case features, not case-specific counterfactual avoidance.

- **Priority 6: Mechanistic mapping via eye-tracking and response time.** Conduct self-paced reading or eye-tracking studies of the Gödel probe in both languages, with response-time collection. If East Asian participants show longer reading times or eye-gaze patterns consistent with re-anchoring to descriptions (revisiting the Gödel-description link), that provides process evidence for the pragmatic re-anchoring hypothesis. Western participants should show faster lock-in to the causal-historical chain.

## Open disagreements in the cell

- **Counterfactual reasoning avoidance as primary mechanism.** The Disruptor proposed that East Asians, reasoning holistically and dialectically, retreat to actual-world descriptions when forced into counterfactual identity-tracking—and this mechanism is *specific* to Gödel because Gödel uniquely combines an identity-falsifying fact with forced counterfactual reasoning. The Architect and Shield both note that (a) Jonah shows cross-cultural effects despite lacking this structure, (b) developmental emergence is too early for mature counterfactual reasoning, and (c) the critical test (removing counterfactual burden) does not exist. The Disruptor acknowledges these weaknesses but argues that effect-size differences between Gödel and Jonah, once quantified, may vindicate case-specificity. **This remains unresolved.**

- **Linguistic pragmatics (bare nouns) vs. perspective-taking ambiguity as the primary driver.** The Institute is split on whether the case-specificity traces to (1) the pragmatic properties of bare noun phrases in article-less languages (Izumi et al.'s confound), which create parsing ambiguity under counterfactual conditions, or (2) the indexical perspective ambiguity in the Gödel vignette itself (whose description is authoritative?), which is not unique to bare nouns but to the *content* of the case. Testing demonstrative effects will settle (1); testing perspective-disambiguation will settle (2). The cell suspects both operate, but does not know the relative weighting.

- **Whether the effect is cultural (learned) or foundational (linguistic).** Early emergence (age 7) is consistent with linguistic scaffolding but could also reflect early internalization of cultural naming conventions. Without the non-verbal child task, this cannot be resolved. The Shield leans toward "foundational + linguistic," the Architect remains agnostic, and the Disruptor leans toward "cultural reasoning style."
