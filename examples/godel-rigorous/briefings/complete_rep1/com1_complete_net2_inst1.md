# Institute Briefing — com1_inst1

**Problem.** What explains why the cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions (East Asians more descriptivist, Westerners more Kripkean) attaches specifically to the Gödel case and is weak, absent, or reversed for other probes like Jonah and Twin Earth?

## Conclusion

The Gödel effect is real and robust, but the three leading mechanistic explanations in the literature—linguistic structure (article marking), analytic-vs.-holistic cognitive style, and pragmatic perspective-taking—are each partially supported and partially falsified by existing evidence. A fourth mechanism, acquiescence response bias triggered by epistemic uncertainty under forced-choice format, remains untested and cannot be ruled out. The effect likely involves interaction effects across linguistic processing, epistemic ambiguity, and culturally-learned response strategies, but the relative contributions and sufficiency of each mechanism remain undetermined pending critical experiments on response format and individual-level response-style measurement.

## Confidence
**0.55** — The Gödel effect replicates and persists even after ambiguity-clarification experiments, but no single proposed mechanism is sufficient, and the critical falsification tests (response-format manipulation, individual acquiescence measurement) have not been conducted.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **The effect is real and case-specific.** Machery et al. (2004) found that East Asian participants (Chinese speakers) gave descriptivist responses to the Gödel case at ~65% versus Western participants at ~35%, while the effect is weak or absent on Jonah and Twin Earth probes. This case-specificity is a hard constraint: it rules out pure cultural-cognitive differences that should generalize across probes.

- **The effect persists after ambiguity clarification.** Sytsma & Livengood (2011) identified epistemic perspective ambiguity in the Gödel case (participants may interpret "John refers to X" as asking whether X is the actual referent vs. the person John takes to be the referent). Their revised version minimized this ambiguity; the cross-cultural difference persisted. This falsifies the hypothesis that perspective ambiguity alone explains the effect.

- **Linguistic structure may play a role, but is not sufficient.** Institute 0's claim that article marking in English forces individuation while Mandarin's bare nominals allow property-based reference is theoretically plausible. However, the claim is undermined by Izumi et al. (2018), who showed that Chinese versions of the probe use multiply-ambiguous bare noun phrases ("Schmidt" vs. "that person called Schmidt"), introducing confounds. Additionally, the linguistic hypothesis does not explain why the effect is specific to Gödel if the mechanism is purely encoding-level.

- **Response-style bias under epistemic uncertainty is theoretically coherent but empirically untested.** Cross-cultural survey methodology establishes that East Asian respondents show more ambivalent and moderate responses, with acquiescence bias (agreeing regardless of content) being 25-30 percentage points higher in collectivist, high power-distance cultures (Smith et al. 2004; Harzing 2006). Forced-choice format under ambiguity should amplify this bias. The Gödel case uniquely involves simultaneous contradictory presuppositions ("Gödel did discover incompleteness; now suppose he didn't"), creating epistemic discomfort. Under binary forced-choice, East Asian participants may select the "safer" description-based answer. Other probes (Jonah, Twin Earth) do not have this epistemic structure, predicting weaker effects. **This mechanism remains completely untested on the Gödel case.**

- **Cross-linguistic variation data is limited and conflicting.** Ding & Liu (2022, cited in Institute 2 briefing) report that native Mandarin administration of the Gödel probe yielded 39% descriptivist responses (vs. 65% in English), interpreted as evidence that English-language phrasing artifacts inflated East Asian descriptivism. However, this is consistent with *either* response-format effects (translation quality, ambiguity framing) *or* native-language pragmatic differences (pro-drop training, etc.). The data do not adjudicate.

- **Cognitive-style hypotheses (analytic vs. holistic thinking) are partially supported but incomplete.** Nisbett's model predicts that holistic-thinking cultures should show different reference intuitions. The case-specific pattern (Gödel ≠ Jonah) is difficult to explain if the mechanism is global cognitive style, but a refined hypothesis—that epistemic uncertainty *interacts* with cognitive style—remains possible. No study has directly measured individual cognitive style alongside Gödel-case responses.

## What would falsify this

- **Response format manipulation.** If the Gödel effect persists or strengthens when participants respond on a 5-point Likert scale ("How confident are you X refers to Y?") instead of binary forced-choice, response-style bias becomes implausible as primary mechanism. Conversely, if the cross-cultural difference shrinks or disappears with Likert format, the acquiescence hypothesis gains decisive support.

- **Individual-level response-style measurement.** If measured acquiescence bias (Leung & Bond Scale, Smith scale) correlates r > 0.3 with Gödel response variation across participants while measured holistic/analytic thinking does not, response-style bias is directly implicated. Absence of correlation falsifies the response-bias mechanism.

- **Extension to non-epistemically-ambiguous cases.** If careful construction of Jonah or Twin Earth variants with identical binary forced-choice framing still shows weak/absent cross-cultural effects (despite clear, non-contradictory presuppositions), this supports the Gödel-specificity hypothesis and challenges general response-bias claims.

- **Native-language replication at scale.** If large-scale native Mandarin administration (n > 500) shows that the Gödel effect *strengthens* rather than weakens (contradicting Ding & Liu), linguistic artifact becomes less plausible. If it weakens substantially, translation/response-style effects become primary suspects.

## Directions for further research

1. **Conduct the response-format experiment.** Administer the Gödel probe in three formats to independent samples: (a) binary forced-choice (original), (b) 5-point Likert scale confidence judgment, (c) open-ended "What does John refer to? Why?" Compare effect sizes across formats and samples. **Priority: Critical.**

2. **Measure individual acquiescence bias.** Administer established response-style scales (Leung & Bond, Smith Acquiescence Scale) to the same participants taking the Gödel probe. Correlate individual acquiescence scores with Gödel responses. Include cognitive-style measures (Nisbett-style holistic thinking scales) as covariates. **Priority: Critical.**

3. **Test case-specificity systematically.** Construct 3-4 additional reference-semantics probes (variants of Jonah, Twin Earth, and novel cases) that either (a) include epistemic ambiguity or (b) do not, held constant across cultures. Predict: epistemically ambiguous cases show cross-cultural effects under binary forced-choice; clear cases do not. **Priority: High.**

4. **Replicate Ding & Liu (2022) with native-language controls.** Conduct large-scale (n > 300 per language group) testing in Mandarin, English, and a third language (e.g., Japanese) using identical-content probes. Control for translation quality, ambiguity framing, and classroom context (collectivist vs. individualist institution norms). **Priority: High.**

5. **Examine linguistic ambiguity in probe construction.** Operationalize Izumi et al.'s critique systematically: code the Mandarin and English probes for bare-noun ambiguities, referential opacity, and presupposition structure. Measure whether ambiguity variance predicts response variation within language groups. **Priority: Medium.**

6. **Develop mechanistic interaction models.** Construct computational or statistical models testing whether linguistic forcing + epistemic uncertainty + response-style bias (as a multiplicative interaction) better predicts observed effect sizes than any single mechanism alone. **Priority: Medium.**
