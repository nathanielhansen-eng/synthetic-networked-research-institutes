# Institute Briefing — com1_inst2

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions (East Asians more descriptivist than Westerners) attach specifically to the Gödel case and not consistently to other reference probes like the Jonah case?

## Conclusion

The cross-cultural Gödel effect is driven by culturally specific perspective-taking strategies under philosophical ambiguity, not by linguistic grammar or deep cognitive style. The Gödel case uniquely contains an irreducible ambiguity about epistemic perspective—whether to adopt the narrator's or protagonist's stance on whom "Gödel" refers—and East Asian and Western participants differ systematically in how they resolve this ambiguity under uncertainty. This accounts for case-specificity: the Jonah case lacks this perspective ambiguity and shows weak or absent cross-cultural effects. The mechanism is pragmatic and cultural, not grammatical or deeply cognitive.

## Confidence
**0.68** — The perspective-taking hypothesis is better supported than competing linguistic and cognitive-style accounts, but the mechanism remains underspecified (which cultural dimensions drive it? how exactly do people resolve the ambiguity?), and case-specificity rests partly on different samples tested for different cases rather than within-subject comparison.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **Case-specificity is real but not explained by linguistic structure.** Machery et al. (2004) report large cross-cultural differences for the Gödel case (Western participants more Kripkean), but the effect is weak or absent for other probes. If the mechanism were classifier grammar or bare-noun licensing (as the Disruptor hypothesized), we should see *stronger* effects in Mandarin Chinese and Japanese speakers. Instead, Ding & Liu (2022) show that the Gödel effect actually *weakens* when the probe is presented in Chinese translation (39% descriptivist in Hong Kong vs. ~65% for East Asians tested in English in the original study). This directly falsifies the linguistic hypothesis: native-language presentation should strengthen, not weaken, a grammatically mediated effect.

- **Philosophical ambiguity, not grammar, explains case-specificity.** Sytsma & Livengood conducted experiments showing that the questions Machery et al. asked were ambiguous—specifically, between asking about speaker's reference (what John intends to refer to) and semantic reference (what "Gödel" literally refers to in the community). Crucially, they also identified a perspective ambiguity: the scenario invites two incompatible readings about whether to adopt the narrator's epistemic stance or John's. Ding & Liu (2022) confirm that there is a descriptive ambiguity in Kripke's Gödel case—people associate different types of descriptions with proper names, such as descriptions of brute facts versus descriptions of social facts—and this ambiguity affects the ratio of Kripkeans in reference. The Gödel case is uniquely perspective-ambiguous; the Jonah case is not. This explains case-specificity directly.

- **The ambiguity is necessary but not sufficient.** Machery conducted clarification experiments isolating the ambiguity, and results suggest that although the perspective ambiguity exists, referential intuitions still vary across and within cultures even after controlling for ambiguity. This indicates that **how people resolve the ambiguity under irreducible uncertainty** differs culturally, not merely whether they perceive ambiguity.

- **Cognitive style (Nisbett's holistic vs. analytic framework) does not predict case-specificity.** If East Asian holism and Western analyticity were the driver, we should see *consistent* cross-cultural differences across all reference probes. Instead, the effect is large for Gödel but weak/absent for Jonah. Machery et al. themselves acknowledge that the attempt to explain cross-cultural variation by appealing to Nisbett-style cognitive differences is insufficient to account for the conflicting empirical patterns across cases.

- **Linguistic evidence does not support the grammatical hypothesis.** Mandarin Chinese lacks overt definiteness articles and uses bare nouns with flexible definite/indefinite/generic interpretation. If bare-noun grammar licenses description-based reference more readily than article-based languages, Mandarin speakers should show *stronger* descriptivist responses than English speakers. The opposite occurs: the effect is *weaker* in native Mandarin than in English translations. Additionally, Hindi, French, and Mongolian speakers show intermediate effects (56%–58% descriptivist), inconsistent with a unified linguistic mechanism based on article systems.

- **Response patterns suggest culturally different resolution of ambiguity, not lexical/grammatical difference.** Responses to the Gödel cases are largely attributable to culturally specific perspective-taking strategies. East Asian participants may show a bias toward *social/collective* interpretations of reference (what the community conventionally calls Gödel), while Western participants prioritize *individual/causal-historical* chains. This is a pragmatic and cultural bias, not a grammatical constraint.

## What would falsify this

- **If the effect remains equally strong across Gödel, Jonah, and other reference probes despite the perspective ambiguity being unique to Gödel**, then the mechanism is global (cognitive or linguistic), not case-specific. Direct within-subject testing of the same participants on multiple cases would establish whether case-specificity is robust.

- **If removing or clarifying the perspective ambiguity eliminates all cross-cultural difference**, then ambiguity (not how people resolve it) is the sole driver. Current evidence shows variation persists after ambiguity clarification, but more rigorous experimental control is needed.

- **If no systematic relationship exists between measured cultural values (individualism, social vs. individual reference norms, naïve epistemology) and the magnitude of the Gödel effect**, then the perspective-taking account loses explanatory grip and suggests an unmeasured mechanism.

- **If the effect strengthens in East Asian participants reading the probe in native language**, then linguistic structure is doing real work. Current evidence contradicts this, but more careful linguistic analysis of how Mandarin, Japanese, and other languages express the relevant distinctions could reveal subtle effects on reference-tracking.

## Directions for further research

- **Within-subject, cross-case design**: Test the same East Asian and Western participants on Gödel, Jonah, and additional reference probes in a single session. This directly measures whether case-specificity is robust and whether it correlates with measured perspective-taking bias or cultural values.

- **Experimental removal of perspective ambiguity**: Design probe variants that explicitly adopt a single epistemic perspective (e.g., "From John's point of view, whom does he think 'Gödel' refers to?" vs. "In the narrator's judgment, whom does 'Gödel' refer to?"). Measure whether cross-cultural differences vanish when perspective is fixed.

- **Linguistic analysis of reference-tracking in typologically diverse languages**: Compare how Mandarin Chinese (classifier, bare-noun language), Japanese (classifier, article-less), English (article-based), and a non-classifier language (e.g., Finnish) express and resolve reference ambiguity in probe variants. Measure whether linguistic encoding predicts response patterns independently of cultural values.

- **Cultural values measurement**: Administer individualism/collectivism scales, naïve epistemology questionnaires, and dialogue/perspective-taking norms assessments to participants, then correlate these with Gödel responses. This would operationalize the "culturally specific perspective-taking" mechanism and test whether it is a real causal factor or merely post-hoc narrative.

- **Longitudinal study of L2 English learners**: Test East Asian L1 speakers with varying L2 English proficiency and length of immersion. If the effect weakens with increased English immersion, this suggests linguistic/pragmatic accommodation. If it persists or strengthens, this suggests deep cultural norms are driving resolution strategy.

## Open disagreements in the cell

The cell agrees that the perspective-taking/ambiguity account is the most plausible current explanation and that both the linguistic and Nisbett cognitive-style hypotheses are inadequately supported. However, one residual tension remains:

**The Disruptor maintained that linguistic structure (classifier grammar, bare-noun systems) should have detectable effects on reference resolution, even if it does not explain the full cross-cultural pattern.** The Disruptor argues that Mandarin Chinese's flexible definite/indefinite interpretation of bare nouns may make reference-tracking *pragmatically dependent* in ways that interact with perspective-taking bias, but that this interaction is subtle and missed by blunt comparisons of aggregate response rates. The Architect contends that if such an effect existed, it should appear as a measurable modulation in native-language testing, and the current evidence (Ding & Liu showing *weaker* effects in Chinese) directly contradicts this.

**Resolution:** Further research should separate grammatical effects on *reference-tracking difficulty* from effects on *resolution strategy under ambiguity*. It is possible that classifier grammar makes perspective ambiguity *easier to sustain* (because bare nouns naturally invite contextual flexibility), thereby *amplifying* perspective-taking bias rather than creating it. This would explain why the effect weakens in native language (because the probe is now *more* pragmatically flexible, making the ambiguity less salient) without requiring that grammar create the cultural difference. This hypothesis merits direct investigation.
