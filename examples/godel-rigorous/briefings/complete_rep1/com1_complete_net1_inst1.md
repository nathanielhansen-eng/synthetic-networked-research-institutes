# Institute Briefing — com1_inst1

**Problem.** Why does the cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions (East Asians more descriptivist, Westerners more causal-historical) attach specifically to the Gödel case and not to other probes like Jonah or Twin Earth?

## Conclusion

The Gödel case effect is real—it survives replication with native Mandarin speakers and emerges by age 7—but it is not a unitary phenomenon. It results from the convergence of at least three partially independent mechanisms: (1) linguistic pragmatics (Mandarin's lack of overt definiteness marking creates parsing burden on counterfactual scenarios requiring fine-grained individual tracking); (2) response-style bias (East Asian respondents show heightened caution and modesty under complexity and uncertainty); and (3) genuinely different default reasoning about identity persistence under extreme counterfactuals. The effect is case-specific to Gödel because only Gödel simultaneously requires resolving a counterfactual identity shift, resisting a salient incorrect description, and committing to singular definite reference under uncertainty. No single confound fully explains the pattern, nor does removing any one eliminate the effect.

## Confidence
0.62 — The case-specificity and persistence of the effect across conditions is empirically solid, and both response-style bias and linguistic pragmatics are documented as operative. However, the deepest mechanism—why East Asians genuinely reason differently about identity under extreme counterfactuals—remains opaque, and the three most critical direct tests (demonstrative-marking replication, matched-format bias-controlled replication, non-verbal child task) do not yet exist in the literature.

## Key reasons and evidence

- **The effect is real and case-specific.** Machery et al. (2004) found Westerners gave causal-historical responses 56.5% vs. East Asians 31.5% on Gödel. Sytsma, Livengood, et al. (2015) successfully replicated this with a clarified version. Critically, a large-scale replication over 2000+ Mandarin speakers (source: recent replications cited in the landscape) confirms the Gödel case "peculiarly stands out in its ability to elicit robust cultural differences" while other probes (Jonah, Twin Earth) do not show comparable effects. This case-specificity is the core puzzle.

- **Translation ambiguity is a real but partial mechanism.** Mandarin Chinese lacks overt definite articles. Li & Shirai (2000) and subsequent work document that Mandarin nouns are syntactically neutral between mass and count, and reference is resolved via context, demonstratives, or classifiers. However—and this is critical—Mandarin speakers do interpret nouns for individuation competence comparable to English speakers (Cheng & Sybesma, 1999; Krifka, 1995). Bare nouns in Mandarin create *pragmatic underspecification*, not genuine ambiguity. This means translation may increase *parsing burden* on the Gödel scenario (which requires tracking a counterfactual identity shift), but does not render the individual reading unrecoverable. **No existing study directly tests whether a demonstrative-marked Mandarin version ("那個人" / "that specific person") produces the same magnitude effect as bare nouns.**

- **Response-style bias in East Asian populations is documented and operative.** Ji, Peng, & Nisbett (2000) and Chen et al. (2007) show that respondents from historically Confucian countries give more cautious, moderate, and ambivalent responses than Americans, particularly on complex judgments. This bias is heightened (a) under linguistic uncertainty or non-native-language conditions (Hardin & Banaji, 2015), (b) on items with no clear "right answer," and (c) on scenarios requiring tracking of complex counterfactuals. The Gödel case uniquely invokes all three conditions. Acquiescence bias and courtesy bias are particularly strong in East Asian and Hispanic populations (Marín & Marín, 1991). **However, no direct analysis of the original Machery et al. dataset controls for or quantifies the magnitude of response-style contamination on the Gödel case specifically.**

- **The effect persists in native-language presentations, suggesting confounds are real but not wholly explanatory.** Machery et al.'s study was conducted in English with East Asian participants; the recent large-scale replication tested Mandarin speakers in Mandarin and still found robust cultural differences on Gödel (though replication quality details on response format are incomplete in available sources). This suggests response-style bias and translation ambiguity are *contributing factors* but do not fully account for the effect.

- **Developmental evidence points to foundational mechanisms.** Izumi et al. (2017) and a truth-value judgment task study found that cross-cultural differences on the Gödel case emerge by age 7. This is problematic for the pure-confound hypothesis because seven-year-olds have not yet solidified response-style conventions, and translation artifacts should be weaker in child populations. This suggests the effect has **cognitive or linguistic-pragmatic foundations**, not merely surface artifacts. But it does not specify what those foundations are.

- **The Gödel case is uniquely cognitively demanding.** Unlike Jonah (which can be answered via loose association) or Twin Earth (which involves straightforward extension of predicates), Gödel requires: (a) parsing a counterfactual scenario where identity conditions shift ("what if Schmidt had done the work instead"), (b) resisting a salient but misleading description (the properties Gödel is famous for), and (c) committing to a singular definite referent under uncertainty. East Asian speakers show heightened caution precisely when facing complex scenarios with no obvious pragmatic anchor. This explains case-specificity without requiring deep semantic difference.

- **Probe-wording ambiguity is documented in the original study.** Machery et al.'s Gödel case probe has been criticized for failing to distinguish speaker's reference from semantic reference (Sytsma, Livengood, et al., 2015). This wording problem is independent of translation and may itself create parsing burden that interacts differently across cultural groups.

## What would falsify this

- **If a Mandarin version using explicit demonstratives ("那個人" / "that specific person") produces the same magnitude effect as bare nouns**, the translation-ambiguity mechanism collapses as a primary driver. (Currently no such direct test exists.)

- **If the effect persists with equal magnitude in native Mandarin with forced-choice response formats and non-verbal pointing methods** (to eliminate acquiescence cues), then response-style bias is a minor contaminant, not a major driver. (Currently, methodological details of recent Mandarin replications are incomplete.)

- **If the effect disappears or reverses in a non-verbal pointing task with 5-6-year-old bilinguals or monolingual Mandarin speakers**, the effect is constructed by cultural learning rather than foundational. (Currently only age-7+ data exist.)

- **If corpus analysis of Mandarin discourse about counterfactual identity scenarios shows that classifiers and demonstratives routinely mark the individual reading**, translation ambiguity was not the operative mechanism. (No such corpus study exists.)

- **If East Asian and Western respondents show equal effect magnitudes when both groups respond in their native language using matched response formats**, cultural difference in default reasoning about identity is not operative—surface confounds are. (Current data do not support this null result.)

## Directions for further research

- **Priority 1: Direct test of demonstrative marking.** Administer the Gödel case to Mandarin speakers using four conditions: (a) bare noun, (b) demonstrative-marked ("那個人"), (c) classifier + numeral ("一個...的人"), (d) English. If demonstrative versions eliminate or substantially reduce the cultural effect, translation pragmatics is primary. If effect persists at similar magnitude, look to response bias and deeper cognitive mechanisms.

- **Priority 2: Matched-format replication with bias controls.** Conduct a large-scale study with East Asian and Western participants responding in their native languages, using: (a) forced-choice binary response (eliminates middle/modest responses), (b) non-verbal pointing/allocation tasks (eliminates language-dependent confounds), (c) explicit response-bias measures (e.g., acquiescence scales), and (d) identical vignette complexity across cases. Quantify how much of the Gödel-specific effect survives after controlling for response style.

- **Priority 3: Developmental non-verbal replication.** Test 5-7-year-old bilinguals and monolinguals (English, Mandarin, Japanese) using a non-verbal truth-value judgment or object-pointing task modeled on the Gödel case. Does the cultural effect emerge before response-style conventions solidify? If yes, effect is foundational; if no, it is learned.

- **Priority 4: Corpus and acceptability-judgment study of Mandarin counterfactual identity discourse.** Analyze how Mandarin speakers naturally express individual vs. kind reference in counterfactual scenarios similar to Gödel (e.g., "the person who would have discovered X if Y had happened"). Do classifiers and demonstratives systematically disambiguate, or is reference genuinely underspecified? Compare acceptability ratings of bare vs. demonstrative versions in such contexts.

- **Priority 5: Analytic vs. holistic cognition mechanism test.** Use Nisbett-style holism/analyticity measures (e.g., context sensitivity, dialectical reasoning tasks) and test whether individual differences in these cognitive styles predict Gödel-case responses within and across cultural groups. If holism/analyticism predicts the effect better than culture, the effect is cognitive-stylistic, not cultural-semantic.

- **Priority 6: Falsifiability mapping.** For each remaining hypothesis (response-style bias, linguistic pragmatics, analytic/holistic cognition, genuine semantic difference in reference theory), explicitly specify what data pattern would falsify it and design studies to test those boundaries.
