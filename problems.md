# Problems for the synthetic research institutes

The orchestrators read the **first `## ACTIVE` block** below as the problem.
To point the cell at something else, either edit the ACTIVE block, demote it to
`## PARKED`, and promote another — or pass `--problem "..."` on the command line.

A good problem here is an **open empirical puzzle answerable with public data**:
something where web search finds the sources and code execution does real
analysis, and where a defensible finding (not just an opinion) is the success
criterion.

## ACTIVE — Why the Gödel case and not the others? (cross-disciplinary, no anchor)

In the experimental philosophy of reference, Machery, Mallon, Nichols & Stich
(2004) reported that East Asian participants were more likely than Western
participants to give **descriptivist** responses to the **Gödel case** (the
Gödel/Schmidt probe from Kripke). The durable puzzle: this cross-cultural
difference attaches **specifically to the Gödel case** and is weak, absent, or
reversed for other probes.

**The explanatory question:** *What explains why the cross-cultural effect
attaches to the Gödel case in particular and not the others?*

You are given **no starting paper** — reconstruct the empirical landscape
yourself via search, and do **not** treat this as a philosophy-only question.
Relevant evidence may live in:

- **linguistics** — definiteness/(in)definiteness marking, bare nominals,
  classifier languages, generics, reference and specificity;
- **psycholinguistics** and sentence processing;
- **cultural & cognitive psychology** — analytic vs. dialectical/holistic
  cognition (Nisbett), response styles, acquiescence bias;
- **translation studies / cross-linguistic survey methodology**;
- **and any other discipline you judge relevant.**

Follow the evidence across fields, name your sources, and clearly distinguish
what is established in the literature from what you are inferring. Adjudicate
among candidate mechanisms, say which the evidence best supports, and state what
would falsify your account.

## PARKED — Why is there a cross-cultural effect for the Gödel case but not for other cases?

In the experimental philosophy of reference, Machery, Mallon, Nichols & Stich
(2004) reported that East Asian participants were more likely than Western
participants to give **descriptivist** responses to the **Gödel case** (the
"Gödel/Schmidt" probe adapted from Kripke), a result taken to threaten the
method of appealing to reference intuitions. The striking and durable finding is
that this cross-cultural difference shows up **specifically for the Gödel case**
and is **weak, absent, or reversed for other probes** — other vignettes, the
Jonah-style cases, causal-historical vs. descriptivist items, epistemic vs.
semantic framings, etc.

**The explanatory question:** *What explains why the cross-cultural effect
attaches to the Gödel case in particular and not to the other cases?* Is it a
genuine cross-cultural difference in reference intuitions, or an artifact of
something specific to the Gödel probe — ambiguity / pragmatics of the question,
an epistemic vs. referential reading, a translation or narrative feature,
response-style differences, individual differences swamping culture, or a
metalinguistic vs. first-order confound? Adjudicate among these, say which the
evidence best supports, and state what would falsify your account.

The attached paper — **Li et al., "Revisiting the Gödel Case"** — is your
starting point; mine it hard, then use web_search to bring in the surrounding
literature (Machery et al. 2004/2010, Sytsma & Livengood, Devitt, Ludwig,
Lam, Beebe & Undercoffer, Cova et al. replication work, etc.). Bring the
evidence and the distinctions, not the vibe.
